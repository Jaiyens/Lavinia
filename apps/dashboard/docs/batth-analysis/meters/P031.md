## (b) RATE SCHEDULE decoded in plain English

- **Inventory rate code:** `AGB` (PG&E AG-B).
- **Billed/latest tariff on every cycle:** **"AGC Ag35+ kW High Use"** (PG&E AG-C, the
  high-use / 35-kW-and-above agricultural demand-metered schedule).

There is a rate-code conflict on this meter: the master spreadsheet says **AG-B**, but the
posted bills are all printed on **AG-C**. That mismatch is itself a finding (see section e).

**What AG-C is, plainly:** AG-C is PG&E's agricultural rate for larger pumps. It is a
**demand-metered, time-of-use** schedule:
- a small fixed **customer charge** billed per day (here **$1.43343/day**),
- a **demand charge** in dollars-per-kW applied to the single highest 15-minute kW the meter
  hit in the cycle (the "Max Demand"), and
- **time-of-use energy** charges (Peak / Partial-Peak / Off-Peak $/kWh) on the kWh actually
  pulled from the grid.

**Why this meter is on a demand rate:** it is a 75 HP pump. A 75 HP motor pulls on the order
of 55-60 kW when running, well above the ~35 kW line that pushes a meter onto the high-use
AG-C/AG-B demand-metered tier. PG&E puts pumps this size on demand rates because their
peak draw is large relative to their energy use.

**The demand $/kW PG&E actually charged here** (read straight off the line items, never
hardcoded):
- 12/2025 sub-period: **$25.62/kW**
- 01/2026 sub-period: **$26.03/kW**
- 02/2026 sub-period: **$26.03/kW**
- 03/2026 sub-period: **$24.95/kW** (post 2026-03-01 mid-cycle rate change)

TOU energy $/kWh windows do not appear as separate priced lines on this meter's recent cycles
because **the meter pulled 0 kWh from the grid** in every reconciled cycle (it is solar-offset
and idle — see section d), so there is nothing to price by TOU window. AG-C's TOU structure is
present in the tariff but produces $0 of energy charge on a zero-import meter.

## (e) EVERY applicable savings finding (full arithmetic + which engine)

All dollar figures below are produced by **deterministic pure functions in

### Finding 1 — The $62,795.65 zero-credit true-up anomaly (NOTABLE, >$500/yr)

- **What:** P031 was charged a **$62,795.65** annual NEM true-up with **zero generation
  credit applied**, despite carrying a solar array and exporting 39,718 kWh over the year.
  The sibling meter **P038** (SA 4699664743, same account, same Big Block solar group) is the
  **proof**: P038's true-up applied generation credit normally, so the mechanism exists and
  P031 should have received an allocation share too. P031 getting $0 of allocated solar
  credit while P038 got its share is the anomaly.
- **Engine:** the allocation share is computed by **`solarAllocation()` in
  `src/lib/energy/solar-allocation.ts`** — a pure, OOM-safe, usage-proportional NEMA
  allocator. By design it computes **SHARES (a %), never a dollar** until a true-up statement
  / Generation Allocation Summary is uploaded (the "honest-blank" law). So the engine can show
  *that P031's share is missing* deterministically, but it will NOT print a recovery dollar
  until the allocation summary is in hand.
- **Arithmetic / the contingency:** the gross exposure is the full **$62,795.65** true-up
  line. Recovery is **$0 to ~$57k, CONTINGENT** on the **Generation Allocation Summary**:
  if the two arrays (840 kW + 1,092 kW = **1,932 kW total**, the verified ground truth — NOT
  12,180 kW) are **fully subscribed / oversubscribed** across the other benefiting meters,
  there is no spare generation to allocate to P031 and the recovery is **zero-sum = $0**. If
  there is unallocated generation, some or most of the $62,795.65 is recoverable. **Do not
  state this as money already banked.** It is a contingent recovery, ceiling ~$57k, floor $0.
- **Data we HAVE:** the printed $62,795.65 true-up line, annual import/export/net kWh, the
  monthly NEM net series, and the P038 sibling proof that allocation works on this group.
- **Data we NEED:** the **Generation Allocation Summary / true-up statement** that shows how
  each array's generation was divided among benefiting meters (`benefitingMeterSaIds` is
  currently empty `[]`, `coverageState: "needs_review"`). Without it the recovery dollar stays
  honest-blank.

### Finding 2 — Rate-code mismatch: inventory says AG-B, bills are AG-C

- **What:** the master spreadsheet lists this SA as **AG-B** while every posted bill is on
  **AG-C "Ag35+ kW High Use."** One of the two is stale. This is exactly the "183 meters on
  mixed legacy/current rates" thesis — a meter that may be mis-rated.
- **Engine:** the alternative-schedule comparison is **`rateLever()` /
  `backTestMeter()` in `src/lib/energy/rate-lever.ts`**, gated by **`bill-verify.ts`**
  (the same recompute machinery, ±`BACK_TEST_BAND_PCT` = 5% band, fail-closed).
- **Arithmetic / why it CANNOT be quoted as a dollar here:** `rateLever` must first reproduce
  the meter's *current* charges from its own billed usage within ±5%. This meter imports
  **0 kWh** and the entire bill is **demand + a NEM true-up**, with **no 15-minute interval
  kWh on file**. Per the ground-truth caveat: **rate optimization needs 15-min interval kWh
  to be trustworthy; bill summaries carry no kWh, and any AG-C→AG-B "savings" the engine
  emits without intervals are sign-ambiguous artifacts.** So this meter's rate finding stays
  **qualitative** ("verify AG-B vs AG-C with PG&E"), with **no dollar**, until intervals land.
- **Data we HAVE:** rate code from inventory (AG-B) vs the four bills' tariff (AG-C), the
  demand $/kW per sub-period.
- **Data we NEED:** **15-minute interval kWh** (Green Button / Share My Data) to back-test the
  current bill and price an alternative schedule honestly.

### Finding 3 — Demand charge on a zero-import meter (the solar-doesn't-cover-demand lesson)

- **What:** the latest cycle billed **$2,867.70 of pure demand charge** (111.52 kW @ ~$25/kW)
  for **0 kWh of net energy**. Solar covered the energy but not the demand kW.
- **Engine:** **`nemDemandInsight()` in `src/lib/energy/solar-nem.ts`** (Lever 4) — pairs the
  reconciled demand cents with the NEM months and surfaces, retrospectively, that solar does
  not lower the demand charge because the peak is set when the panels are near-off.
  `maxDemand()` in `demand.ts` is what derives the kW from intervals when present.
- **Arithmetic:** demand component of the latest bill = $1,711.73 + $1,112.97 = **$2,824.70**;
  annualized across the demand-bearing cycles this is the bulk of the meter's non-true-up cost.
  This is a *legibility/insight* finding (explains the bill), not a "switch and save" dollar —
  the operational fix (shift the peak) only helps if there is schedule slack, which a vineyard
  pump in season may not have.
- **Data we HAVE:** demand kW + $/kW per cycle.
- **Data we NEED:** 15-minute intervals to confirm *when* the 111.52 kW peak was set (is it in
  the 5-8pm rate-peak window) before recommending any peak shift.
