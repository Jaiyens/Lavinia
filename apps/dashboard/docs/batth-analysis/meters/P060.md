What AG-A1 is, in operator English:
- It is the **agricultural small-service TOU schedule, low-use flavor** — PG&E's "Ag<35 kW Low Use" rate. It is built for a meter with **few operating hours / low load factor**: it has **no demand charge at all**, recovering everything through a tiny daily customer charge plus the *highest* per-kWh energy of the four AG sub-rates.
- **Customer (meter) charge:** **$0.68895/day** (≈ $20.96/month), the AG-A1 secondary-voltage rate — exactly what this bill printed, confirming the AG-A1 mapping against the real bill.
- **Demand charge ($/kW):** AG-A1 has **NO published max-demand charge and NO peak-window demand charge** — it is energy-only-plus-customer. (See the anomaly in (c): this bill nonetheless prints TWO demand-flavored lines totaling $52.92, which is not consistent with a pure AG-A1 tariff and is the meter's only real finding.)
- **Energy ($/kWh), TOU windows:** AG-A1 is the *highest-energy / lowest-fixed* corner. The dated card on this winter bill: **peak ≈ $0.345/kWh, off-peak ≈ $0.316/kWh** (pre-03/01), stepping to **peak ≈ $0.340, off-peak ≈ $0.311** (post-03/01). **Peak window is 5–8pm year-round** (rest off-peak in v1's two-bucket model); ag summer = May–October. AG-A1 energy is the *highest* of the four sub-rates precisely because it recovers everything through energy (no demand charge).
- **Why this meter is on AG-A1:** a farm shop pulls modest total kWh (809 kWh this cycle) and a small peak (4.6 kW), far under the 35 kW "large" break. AG-A1's near-zero fixed cost and zero demand charge is the *cheapest* place for a tiny, intermittent building load — you eat the higher per-kWh energy on a small kWh base. **This is a plausibly-correct, even near-optimal, rate assignment** for a low-use shop.

### 1. Rate optimization — `rateOptimization(input)` in `src/lib/energy/rate-compare.ts` (also `rateLever` in `rate-lever.ts`)

- **What it does:** deterministically maps `AGA1 → AG-A1`, sizes the meter (`sizeClassFor`: 4.596 kW peak → **small / <35 kW**), back-tests this cycle against the published card to prove it reproduces the real bill (`reproductionError` within tolerance), then prices the same usage on every **eligible** target (same size class, agricultural, non-legacy, different family) and quotes the cheapest only if it clears the **$200/yr floor AND 3% of the bill** (`material` gate). Fail-closed: no trustworthy reproduction → no "switch and save" claim.
- **Result for this meter: NO defensible rate-switch finding.**
  - The only in-size-class alternative is **AG-A2** (the high-use sibling). AG-A1 and AG-A2 share the **same** $0.68895/day customer charge; the trade is: AG-A1 has **no demand charge** but the *highest* energy, AG-A2 adds an **$11.79/kW max-demand charge** but cheaper energy. For a meter with a **tiny peak (4.6 kW) and only 809 kWh**, AG-A1's "no demand charge" wins decisively — moving to AG-A2 would *add* an ~$46–$54 demand charge (4.6 kW × ~$11.79) to save only a few dollars of energy on 809 kWh. So the lever returns **no savings** here; AG-A1 is already the cheaper of the two.
  - The lever will not propose AG-B/AG-C: this meter is size-class **small** and its 4.6 kW peak is far below the 35 kW break (and the one-way ratchet keeps it there). Correct — a 4.6 kW load does not belong on a 35-kW pump rate.
- **Data we HAVE:** one reconciled winter cycle with real TOU energy (809.5 kWh) and a real peak (4.596 kW).
- **Data we NEED for a full-year cross-check:** **15-minute interval kWh + a summer cycle**, to confirm the load never approaches 35 kW and to price a complete year. With one winter bill the engine can only conclude "AG-A1 looks correct, AG-A2 would be worse." **No >$500/yr rate finding** — this is a ~$2–4k/yr meter and no plausible AG-family switch moves $500.
