## (b) RATE SCHEDULE — decoded in plain English

**On the rate:** **AG-4C** (PG&E billing code `AG4C`), a **legacy large time-of-use agricultural
schedule**. AG-4 is a closed (legacy) Time-of-Use Ag rate; the "C" variant is the three-period
summer / high-use flavor.

- **Customer charge:** **$2.15003 per meter per day** (the highest customer charge in the AG-4
  family — Rate C,F). On a ~30-day cycle that is roughly **$64.50/month**, which is exactly what
  this meter prints (see section c). For a 365-day year this fixed charge alone is
  **$2.15003 x 365 = $784.76/yr**.
- **Demand $/kW:** AG-4C carries a legacy max-demand charge and a **Summer Peak-Demand charge
  assessed on the noon-6 pm window** (the legacy peak). It is a demand-bearing rate when the pump
  actually runs. This meter is dead, so it pays **$0 demand** this cycle.
- **TOU windows (legacy AG-4-C, three summer periods):** Summer **Peak 12 pm-6 pm Mon-Fri**;
  Summer **Part-Peak 8:30 am-12 pm and 6 pm-9:30 pm Mon-Fri**; Summer **Off-Peak 9:30 pm-8:30 am
  plus all weekend**; Winter Part-Peak 8:30 am-9:30 pm Mon-Fri, Winter Off-Peak otherwise. The
  defining feature is the **noon-6 pm peak**, NOT the modern 5-8 pm peak.

**WHY this meter is on AG-4C (the important part):** P017 is **NEM2-paired solar** (group 4433).
The legacy noon-6 pm peak window **overlaps midday solar production**, so the solar offsets the
most expensive hours. The modern Schedule AG peak (5-8 pm) no longer overlaps solar. The
rate-arbitrage lens (`findings-rate-schedule-arbitrage.json`) explicitly classifies these legacy
solar pumps as **intentional, not a rate error**: moving them to modern Schedule AG early would
DESTROY solar value. They force-transition to Schedule AG only when solar-legacy eligibility lapses
(no later than 7/31/2027); at that forced transition AG-4C -> AG-C. So the rate is deliberately
held, not a mistake to fix today.
