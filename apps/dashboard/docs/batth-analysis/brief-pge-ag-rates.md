# PG&E Agricultural & Business Electric Rate Schedules — Reference Brief (2025–2026)

**Scope.** Current PG&E agricultural and small-business electric tariffs relevant to a Central Valley pump-heavy farm: the default Schedule AG family (AG-A1, AG-A2, AG-B, AG-C), the legacy large TOU schedules AG-4 and AG-5 (incl. AG-4C, AG-5B, AG-5C), legacy AG-1, the high-voltage agricultural code **HAGC**, and the general-service business rates **B-1** and **B-19**.

**Effective date of all dollar figures below:** the current general rate-change cycle, **Advice 7846-E, effective March 1, 2026** (with a few component sub-rows effective Jan 1, 2026 per Advice 7797-E). All figures are PG&E *bundled* total rates (generation + delivery) unless noted. Verify against the live tariff book before billing-grade use; PG&E re-prices these multiple times per year.

**Two structural facts that drive everything:**
1. **The 35 kW cutoff splits the AG family.** Schedule AG (the modern default) has four sub-rates. AG-A1 / AG-A2 are for installations **under 35 kW**; AG-B / AG-C are for installations **35 kW or more**. ([Schedule AG, Sheet 2](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_AG.pdf))
2. **Within each size class, "1/B = low-use" vs "2/C = high-use."** AG-A1 and AG-B are tuned for *lower* load factor / fewer operating hours (lower demand charge, higher energy charge). AG-A2 and AG-C are tuned for *higher* load factor / more operating hours (higher demand charge, lower energy charge). ([Schedule AG, Sheet 2](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_AG.pdf))

## 1. Schedule AG (the modern default) — AG-A1 / AG-A2 / AG-B / AG-C

Source: **[PG&E Electric Schedule AG (ELEC_SCHEDS_AG.pdf)](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_AG.pdf)**, Advice 7846-E, eff. March 1, 2026. Cross-checked against the [3CE PG&E-territory rate sheet eff. Feb 15, 2026](https://3cenergy.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/PGE-Agricultural-Website-Rate-Sheet-v29-2026.02.15.pdf).

### Eligibility / applicability
- Served on Schedule AG if **≥70% of annual metered energy is agricultural end-use** (growing crops, raising livestock, irrigation pumping, or production for sale).
- **< 35 kW → AG-A1 (low use) or AG-A2 (high use).** Applies to single-motor installs rated < 35 kW and multi-load installs aggregating < 35 kW.
- **≥ 35 kW → AG-B (med use) or AG-C (high use).** A customer with reported rated capacity ≥ 35 kW, **or** with a *metered* max demand of **35 kW or greater in any month in the most recent 12 months**, is eligible for (and gets defaulted toward) AG-B/AG-C.
- Interval (remotely-read) meter required.

### Time periods (all four sub-rates, year-round, every day incl. weekends/holidays)
- **Summer = June 1 – September 30. Winter = October 1 – May 31.**
- **Peak: 5:00 p.m. – 8:00 p.m. every day. Off-Peak: all other hours.** No partial-peak period on the modern AG rates.
- Maximum demand = highest 15-minute average in the month. Maximum-peak-period demand = highest 15-minute average *during the 5–8 p.m. peak window*.

**Key reading of this table:**
- **AG-A1 has NO demand charge at all** — just a flat ~$0.69/day customer charge plus per-kWh energy. AG-A1's energy rates are the *highest* of the four (peak-summer $0.516/kWh) because it recovers everything through energy.
- **AG-A2 has a Max-Demand charge but NO peak-demand charge** ($11.79/kW secondary on the single monthly maximum), with lower energy than AG-A1.
- **AG-B has NO demand charge** (like AG-A1 but for the 35+ class) — energy-only recovery, peak-summer $0.513/kWh.
- **AG-C is the demand-heavy rate:** a **Max-Demand charge ($21.43/kW secondary)** *plus* a **Summer Peak-Demand charge of $29.92/kW** assessed on the highest 15-min demand during the 5–8 p.m. window in summer, plus the highest monthly customer charge (~$43.58/mo). In exchange, AG-C's *energy* rates are by far the lowest (peak-summer only $0.213/kWh). AG-C is the only AG sub-rate with the **Demand Charge Rate Limiter** ($0.50/kWh blended cap excluding the customer charge), which protects spiky, low-energy meters from runaway demand charges.

### HAGC — the high-voltage AG-C code
**HAGC is not a separate published tariff.** It is PG&E's billing code for **Schedule AG rate AG-C taken at *transmission* (high) voltage** (the "H" prefix = high/transmission voltage; the same farm's transmission shops/offices show as HAGA1, HAGA2, HAGB, HB1). On HAGC the demand charges drop to the **Transmission-voltage rows of the AG-C column: Max Demand $6.45/kW** and the **Summer Peak Demand $29.92/kW** (the peak-demand charge is the same $29.92 across all voltage classes), and the customer charge and energy rates are the AG-C values above. Transmission service is only available where the customer is served directly off PG&E's transmission system (large solar-paired pump banks, e.g. the 840 kW / 1,092 kW wells in this farm's fleet).

## 2. Legacy large TOU agricultural schedules — AG-4 and AG-5

These are **closed legacy schedules** (closed to new enrollment since March 1, 2020; remaining customers default to Schedule AG). They survive only for (a) behind-the-meter **solar-legacy-TOU** customers (kept until 10 yr after permission-to-operate, no later than **July 31, 2027**, or Dec 31, 2027 for public agencies), (b) "highly impacted" customers, and (c) customers still without a remotely-read interval meter. **AG-5 is scheduled to expire in 2027.** ([Schedule AG-5, Sheets 1–4](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_AG-5.pdf))

> **Why this farm still cares:** its solar pumps (NEM2 paired) are parked on AG-4C, AG-5B, AG-5C precisely to hold the *legacy TOU window* (noon–6 pm peak), which front-loads the solar production curve against peak. When solar-legacy eligibility lapses (2027), every one of these meters force-transitions to Schedule AG (AG-5B/C → **AG-C**; AG-5A/D ≥35 kW → AG-B; AG-5A/D <35 kW → AG-A2) with the late **5–8 p.m.** peak that no longer overlaps midday solar.

## 3. Business / general-service rates — B-1 and B-19

These are **not** agricultural rates (a meter with ≥70% ag use is required to be on an AG schedule). They appear on farms for **non-pumping loads**: shops, offices, dehydrators, cold storage. On this farm: the dehydrator and a ranch office are on **B-1**; the high-voltage office is **HB1** (B-1 at transmission voltage).

Source: **[Schedule B-1, Sheets 1 & 3](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_B-1.pdf)**.

## 4. Rules for switching rate schedules

- **Minimum term / "stickiness":** Schedule AG service is provided for a **minimum of 12 months**; a customer may be required to sign a 1-year contract, after which it continues until cancelled. B-19's voluntary version "will remain in effect for at least twelve consecutive months before another schedule change is made." Practically, **PG&E limits discretionary rate changes to roughly once every 12 months** per meter. ([Schedule AG, Sheet 8](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_AG.pdf); [B-19, Sheet 17](https://www.pge.com/tariffs/assets/pdf/tariffbook/ELEC_SCHEDS_B-19.pdf))
- **You may always elect any rate you are eligible for.** Within Schedule AG a customer "may elect any rate for which they are eligible," including the optional flexible-off-peak Schedule AG-F. Moving *between* AG-A1/A2/B/C is an election, not a tariff change, as long as the size class fits.
- **The 35 kW line is a one-way ratchet toward B/C.** If a meter's *metered* max demand hits **35 kW or more in any single month over the trailing 12 months**, it becomes eligible for — and PG&E will default it to — AG-B/AG-C, even if its nameplate is under 35 kW. To get *back* to AG-A1/AG-A2 the meter must stay **under 35 kW for the trailing 12 months**. So a single demand spike can strand a meter on the 35+ class for up to a year.
- **B-1 → B-19 escalation:** crossing 75 kW (B-1 cap) or 500 kW (B-19 mandatory floor) for 3 consecutive months forces an upward transition at the next November (B-1) or 3-month-trigger (B-19) review, with 45-day notice.
- **PDP (Peak Day Pricing)** is the default for large ag/C&I meters ≥200 kW for 3 consecutive months; opt-out to a plain TOU rate is allowed.

## 5. The core arbitrage — when an AG-C meter belongs on AG-A1/AG-A2

This is the decision the farm's pump fleet turns on. **AG-C trades a big fixed cost (≈$43.58/mo customer charge + a $29.92/kW summer-peak-demand charge + a $21.43/kW max-demand charge) for the cheapest energy.** AG-A1 trades almost no fixed cost (≈$0.69/day, **zero demand charge**) for the most expensive energy. AG-A2 sits between (a max-demand charge but no peak-demand charge).

**The break-even is almost entirely about demand (kW), not energy (kWh):**

- **AG-C only "wins" when the meter pushes serious kW** — because that is the only way to dilute the $29.92/kW summer peak-demand charge and the ~$43.58/mo customer charge across enough cheap-energy kWh. A meter that *legitimately* sits in the 35+ kW class and runs many hours (high load factor, e.g. a 100+ kW pump running long irrigation sets) recovers those fixed/demand dollars and benefits from AG-C's $0.21/kWh vs AG-A1's $0.52/kWh peak energy.
- **AG-A1/AG-A2 win whenever the meter's actual demand is small or its hours are few** — i.e. low load factor. With no demand charge (AG-A1) the bill is just a tiny daily charge + energy, so a pump that barely runs, or runs well under 35 kW, pays almost nothing in fixed cost and is never exposed to a $29.92/kW peak-demand hit.

**The trap:** PG&E *defaults* a meter to AG-B/AG-C the moment its metered demand touches 35 kW in any month — but it does **not** automatically demote it back to AG-A1/AG-A2 when the load shrinks (e.g. after a motor downsizing, a VFD retrofit, a solar pairing that clips grid-draw demand, or simple seasonal idling). So a large population of meters sit on **AG-C paying ~$43.58/mo + demand charges while their real demand is now well under 35 kW** and they would pay only ~$0.69/day with **no demand charge** on **AG-A1**. For a low-utilization meter, the AG-C customer-charge premium alone is ~$23/mo ($276/yr) before a single kW of demand charge — and the demand charges are where the real money is.

**Concrete test for each meter:** pull the trailing-12-month interval data and compute the **maximum 15-minute demand**. If that max is **comfortably below 35 kW** (and especially if peak-window 5–8 p.m. demand is small), the meter is eligible for AG-A1/AG-A2 and almost certainly cheaper there:
- If the meter has **low annual operating hours / low total kWh** → **AG-A1** (no demand charge; you eat the higher energy rate on few kWh).
- If it has **higher operating hours but still <35 kW** → **AG-A2** (a modest $11.79/kW max-demand charge buys materially lower energy than AG-A1).

## 6. One-paragraph summary — the single most exploitable arbitrage

For a farm with **many low-demand pumps stranded on high-use schedules (AG-C / AG-5C / AG-4C / HAGC)**, the highest-leverage move is a **fleet-wide "demote to AG-A1" sweep**: pull each meter's trailing-12-month interval data, find the maximum 15-minute demand, and re-elect every meter whose true max demand now sits **below 35 kW** down to **AG-A1** (no demand charge, ~$0.69/day) — or **AG-A2** if it runs many hours. These meters were auto-defaulted up to the 35+ kW class by a transient demand spike or by a historic nameplate, and PG&E never demotes them back automatically, so they are quietly paying the ~$43.58/mo AG-C customer charge **plus** a $21.43/kW max-demand charge **plus** the punishing **$29.92/kW summer 5–8 p.m. peak-demand charge** on loads that no longer justify any demand charge at all. On a low-load-factor pump the swing is large: eliminate the entire demand-charge column, cut the fixed charge by ~$23/mo, and accept higher per-kWh energy on a small kWh base — typically net savings of hundreds to low-thousands of dollars per meter per year, multiplied across dozens of such pumps. The only guardrails: (1) the 35 kW eligibility is a trailing-12-month ratchet, so confirm no month breached 35 kW (a single spike disqualifies AG-A for a year), and (2) the change sticks for ~12 months, so don't demote a meter whose demand is about to climb again seasonally.
