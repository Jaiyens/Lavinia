# Terra Purchasing Agent: the buyer that works for your farm, not the store

## A California grower can now see every line of input spend against the real market band, catch the rebates they were owed and never credited, and get a verified savings number built from their own invoices, paying Terra only out of what it saves.

**Fresno, California, October 1, 2027** - Terra today opened its Purchasing Agent to California almond and tree-nut growers. It is the second tool in Terra's operating system for California farmers, built on the same idea that runs the PG&E energy tool: make the mess legible first, then find the money hiding in it. Inputs are the largest controllable cost on a farm. Crop inputs (chemicals, fertilizer, seed) were $72.2B, or 28.6 percent of crop-farm total expenses, in 2024 ([USDA via AFBF](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/declining-farm-economy-continues-to-pressure-profitability)). The first version makes the buying legible: it reads a farm's crop plan and its past invoices, forecasts what it will need to buy and when, shows every line against a normalized per-unit market band, and audits the rebate and prepay paperwork against what was actually invoiced to surface dollars the grower earned but was never credited. Every finding is a verified dollar number, traceable line by line, built from the grower's own invoices. Live cross-supplier scouting and a one-tap purchase order are the next layers, coming as Terra earns supplier participation; the day-one product needs no dealer to cooperate to put dollars on the table. Terra is the grower's agent, not a store, and it gets paid only from the savings it proves.

Growers buy almost nothing direct. Inputs flow through a consolidated dealer and co-op channel where seven retailers control roughly 70 percent of crop inputs and services ([CropLife](https://www.croplife.com/croplife-top-100/the-big-seven-of-the-croplife-100-the-evolution-of-americas-largest-ag-retailers/)), and where the real net price only appears after year-end rebates and program pricing settle ([CropLife: Marketing/Rebate Programs](https://www.croplife.com/management/marketingrebate-programs/)). The result is that two growers buy the same active ingredient at wildly different prices and neither can tell. The price-transparency work that opened this market reports a roughly 15 percent average gap between list and price actually paid and much wider variation farm to farm, up to 283 percent for the same chemistry in 2023 and 468 percent in 2024, though these are FBN-sourced and directional, not independently audited, and FBN's own data admits the below-average half of buyers captured only about 14 percent off list, not the headline figure ([FBN 2024 report via AgWeb](https://www.agweb.com/news/business/fbn-releases-its-2024-ag-chemical-price-transparency-report)). The numbers are soft, which is exactly why we build our own. Half of farmers say they cannot get pricing data and 41 percent cannot compare products at all ([The Daily Scoop](https://www.thedailyscoop.com/news/retail-business/farm-business-2026-relationship-first-digital-convenience-second)). The squeeze makes it bite: input prices sit 20 to 40 percent above pre-2021 levels while 2025 per-acre margins went negative for every major row crop, and almond net returns above total costs swung from a +$205/acre gain in 2019 to a roughly $4,280/acre loss in 2024 ([AFBF specialty](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/specialty-crops-mounting-cost-pressure-limited-risk-protection)). That loss is net return against a full mature-orchard cost base of roughly $5,000 to $6,500/acre (UC Davis), not a single line item; the fertilizer and crop-protection spend the Purchasing Agent works on is the ~$800/acre slice of that base where the price discovery is worst, which is why even a modest percentage recovered there is real money in a year this deep underwater.

What changes for the grower is that the buying stops being a black box. They open Terra and see their own season laid out: here is what each block needs, here is what you paid last year, here is the per-unit market band for the same product, here is the same active ingredient as a generic, here is the rebate you earned but were never credited, here is the prepay window and what it is worth this year. Every recommendation is one situation, one action, the dollar impact, a one-tap response, and an after-the-fact result once the invoice posts. The grower keeps their dealer and their PCA. The agent simply makes sure they are buying from a position of knowledge instead of from a position of being a price taker.

> "Farmers told us the same thing they told us about their energy bills: I am sure there is money in here, but I cannot see it, and I do not have time to learn it line by line. We built the Purchasing Agent so the grower never has to. It does the line-by-line work, in plain language, and it only earns when the grower earns. We do not sell the inputs, so we have no reason to point you anywhere but the cheapest honest option, including the dealer you already trust."
> - Jaiyen Shetty, Terra

### How It Works

A grower connects the Purchasing Agent the same way they connected the energy tool: it already knows their ranches, blocks, crops, and entities from Terra. They upload or forward last season's input invoices, by photo, PDF, or a forward from the dealer's email. That is the only setup.

From there the launched version does three things, in order, and shows its work at every step:

1. It forecasts the season. Acres times crop times program times growth stage becomes a per-product bill of materials and a buying calendar, so the grower sees what is coming before the dealer's order sheet arrives.
2. It makes the spend legible. Every past line gets normalized to a per-unit, same-active-ingredient comparison against the market band, so "you paid $X versus the median $Y" is visible at a glance, by SKU, dealer, ranch, and entity.
3. It audits the paperwork. The agent reconciles every invoice against every rebate tier, threshold, and early-fill milestone to catch dollars the grower earned but was never credited, and models prepay timing against price risk, storage, and the tax window. After the next bill posts, it shows predicted versus actual on every finding, the same close-the-loop the energy tool does.

Two more capabilities are coming as Terra earns supplier participation, and we are plain that they are not in this first release. Live scouting will pull quotes across local dealers, co-ops, and generic-equivalent channels and line them up in the same units, with the grower's existing dealer invited to quote too. A one-tap purchase order will then let the grower approve a buy bounded by dollar caps they set, visible to their PCA or dealer rep, with delivery tracked to the ranch. We ship those once enough suppliers in a grower's area will compete on a transparent number; the day-one legibility and rebate audit put dollars on the table without waiting on any of it.

> "I run six entities and I buy from four different places. I never had one screen that showed me what I was actually spending across all of it, never mind whether it was a fair price. For the first time I could see every line on one screen and check the math line by line, the same way I do in Excel. Now when my dealer sends the spring sheet, I can read it against the band and send it back with three lines changed."
> - illustrative grower account, composite of San Joaquin Valley almond operations

### Getting Started

Any California almond or tree-nut grower already using Terra's energy tool can turn on the Purchasing Agent from the same home screen. New growers can start with the Purchasing Agent alone. There is no membership fee. The agent works for free until it proves savings on the grower's own past invoices, and Terra is paid only as a share of the savings it realizes going forward. The grower keeps their dealers, keeps their PCA, and keeps final say on every order.

## Customer FAQ

### Q: How is this different from FBN, which already shows me price transparency?

A: FBN is a seller. It runs a marketplace, FBN Direct, and it makes money on the transaction, so it will not show you a competitor's lower quote or audit the rebate you earned from a product it did not sell you. We do not sell inputs at all. We are your agent. That means we can scout every channel, including the dealer you already buy from, and line them all up honestly. Two other differences matter for you specifically. FBN benchmarks against national, row-crop averages, which are thin for California almonds and tree nuts. We are built for your crops and your per-acre economics. And FBN shows you what other farmers paid in the past. We forecast what you will need to buy this season from your own crop plan, scout live prices when it is time to buy, and close the loop after the invoice posts. FBN does have AI (an advisor called Norm, built on GPT-3.5, launched in 2023 ([AgFunder](https://agfundernews.com/why-farmers-business-network-launched-norm-an-ai-advisor-for-farmers-built-on-chatgpt))). What does not exist anywhere, from FBN or anyone else, is an independent agent that forecasts, scouts, audits, and prepares the order on your side of the table.

### Q: Are you going to push me to ditch my dealer and my PCA?

A: No, and we think a tool that tried to would fail. Your PCA is the legal gatekeeper for crop-protection recommendations in California ([California Ag Today](https://californiaagtoday.com/pest-control-adviser-certified-crop-adviser-programs/)), and the dealer agronomist is in nearly every grower's circle of trusted advisors ([GROWERS](https://growers.ag/blog/how-ag-retailers-can-engage-with-a-farmers-circle-of-trusted-advisors/)). We arm you and them, we do not replace them. Your dealer is invited to quote alongside everyone else, and your PCA can see every recommendation. Most growers use what we surface to have a better conversation with the dealer they already like, and the dealer often matches the number. We will be straight with you, though: some dealers will not want to compete on a number they cannot hide behind, and a few may slow-walk a quote or decline to play. That is their choice, and if it happens you lose nothing and keep full control, because the legibility and the rebate audit run on your own invoices and do not need any dealer to cooperate. Loyalty here is about the relationship, not the brand. About a third of farmers call themselves brand-loyal and 60 percent would switch brands for a 10 percent discount ([The Grower](https://thegrower.org/news/price-performance-loyalty-which-one-works-you)). We just make sure the relationship is not costing you money you cannot see.

### Q: What does it cost, and what is the catch on "free"?

A: There is no membership fee. We start by auditing your past invoices for free and showing you what we would have saved. Going forward we are paid only as a share of the savings we actually realize for you, gain-share. If we do not save you money, you do not pay us. The catch you should watch for is the opposite of most software: our incentive is to find you real dollars, because that is the only way we get paid. Later we may add transaction or financing options, but those will always be opt-in, and the savings-share stays the core of how we make money on day one.

### Q: What happens to my invoices and my crop plan? Who sees my data?

A: Your invoices and crop plan are used to do your work and to compute an anonymized market band that helps every grower, the same way the energy tool works. Your numbers are never shown to another grower with your name on them, and they are never sold to a dealer or manufacturer. We are honest that the market band only works because growers share data into it, so we will always tell you exactly what is pooled (anonymized per-unit prices) and what is not (anything that identifies your farm). We never store dealer or financial login credentials anywhere the system or our team can read them, the same discipline we hold on the energy side.

### Q: I have been burned by ag-tech that overpromised. Why should I believe your savings number?

A: Because we show you the savings on your own past invoices before you pay anything, and we show predicted versus actual after every order posts. We are not asking you to trust a vendor headline. The savings figures floating around this market (the 15 to 40 percent claims, the 283 percent variation in 2023 and 468 percent in 2024, the energy tool's 40 percent on one pump) are all vendor self-reported and not independently audited (estimate). We treat those as the reason to look, not as a promise. Your number is your number, computed from your invoices, and you can check our math line by line in a table you can export to a spreadsheet.

### Q: What if I buy something the agent did not plan for, or I need to buy fast?

A: The forecast is a starting point, not a cage. You can buy anything, any time, through your normal channels, and the agent will still capture the invoice, check the price against the band, and catch the rebate. The one-tap order and the scouting are there when you want leverage; they are never required. If you need product tomorrow, the agent will show you who has it and at what price quickly, but you are never blocked waiting on it.

### Q: Does it work for more than crop protection and fertilizer?

A: At launch we focus on the lines with the most hidden money and the worst price discovery: crop protection, fertilizer and nutrition, and seed. Fuel is comparatively transparent (off-road diesel pricing is straightforward and bought on keep-full delivery), so we surface it for completeness but do not lead with it. Equipment parts and repair are a real margin pool ([PIRG](https://pirg.org/updates/dealership-consolidation-raising-repair-costs-farmers/)) and a candidate for later, not day one.

### Q: What if Terra shuts down or gets acquired? I do not want to be stranded.

A: Your data is yours and exportable at any time, to a spreadsheet, no lock-in. We are building this as a durable tool with a real revenue model (savings-share), not a land-grab that needs a fire hose of funding to survive. We have watched the cautionary tales in this space, including a marketplace that reportedly fell roughly 94 percent from its peak (a figure the company disputes; estimate) ([AgFunder](https://agfundernews.com/no-comment-from-indigo-ag-on-valuation-nose-dive-report)). Our answer is to stay capital-disciplined, lead with software and savings rather than balance-sheet-heavy logistics, and earn our keep from realized dollars.

## Internal FAQ

### Q: Channel conflict is the obvious landmine. The Big Seven control ~70 percent of inputs and the dealer relationship is sticky. What stops the channel from freezing us out the way they fought FBN?

A: This is the central risk and we will not pretend otherwise. FBN drew incumbent hostility, including a now-closed Canadian Competition Bureau probe, precisely because it set itself up as a rival seller. Our structural defense is that we are not a seller. We do not take the dealer's transaction; we invite the dealer to quote and often hand them the order. An agent that brings a dealer a ready-to-fill, correctly-rebated PO from a grower who was going to buy anyway is a customer, not a threat, for the dealers willing to compete on price. The honest exposure remains: dealers who price-discriminate by farm size and sophistication lose margin when their pricing becomes legible, and some will retaliate by slow-walking quotes or refusing to participate. Mitigation is sequencing. We lead with the two capabilities that deliver dollars without needing any dealer to cooperate: legibility on the grower's own invoices, and the grower-side rebate and prepay audit. Those work on day one against data the grower already has. Live cross-supplier scouting deepens as we earn supplier participation, and we start where we have the most grower density to make quoting worth a dealer's time.

### Q: Trust to delegate spend is the steepest climb. Why will a skeptical, low-software-literacy grower let an AI touch their buying?

A: They will not, on day one, and we are not asking them to. The product is human-in-the-loop by default: it forecasts, scouts, audits, and negotiates, then presents a one-tap PO with dollar caps the grower sets and full PCA visibility. True delegated auto-buy is a later setting, earned. The data is sobering and we treat it as a design constraint, not a footnote: only about 27 percent of consumers trust fully autonomous AI for financial transactions (cross-industry, not farm-specific; estimate), and 53 percent of North American farmers are very concerned about clear ROI before buying ag-tech ([McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/agriculture/our-insights/global-farmer-insights-2024)). The path to delegation is the same one the energy tool walks: prove retrospective accuracy on past invoices, then close the predicted-versus-actual loop on live orders, then offer one-tap with caps, and only much later, with a track record, offer true auto-buy. Trust is earned in dollars, in that order.

### Q: Supplier liquidity. The scouting and negotiation value depends on enough suppliers willing to quote. California specialty is thinner than the Midwest row-crop footprint FBN and GROWERS built around. How is the marketplace not dead on arrival?

A: We decouple the value from the marketplace deliberately. The rebate audit and the price-band legibility produce hard dollars with zero suppliers in the network, because they run on the grower's own invoices and the anonymized band we build from our own base. That is the day-one product, and it is defensible without supplier density. The live-quote network is the second layer, and we build it where we already have grower concentration (the California almond and tree-nut base Terra reaches through the energy tool), because density on the buy side is what makes quoting worth a dealer's time. We do not need national coverage to be useful; we need enough local dealers per quoting event for a Batth-scale operation to feel competition. The honest risk is that some regions or some product lines never reach quoting density. We accept that and lead with the audit-and-band value there.

### Q: Gain-share unit economics. If we only get paid from realized savings, can this actually make money, or are we building a thin-margin trap?

A: The per-acre math supports it but the model has to stay disciplined. UC Davis puts 2024 Sacramento Valley almond fertilizer and crop-protection lines at well over $800/acre ([UC Davis 2024 Sample Costs to Produce Almonds](https://coststudyfiles.ucdavis.edu/2024/07/09/2024SacValleyAlmonds7.5.24.%20Final%20draft.pdf)). If the agent captures even 5 to 10 percent of that line, that is roughly $40 to $80/acre of grower value; a savings-share could plausibly capture $5 to $20/acre as revenue (estimate). Across even 100,000 to 200,000 almond acres that is roughly $0.5M to $4M of early annual revenue from almonds alone (estimate), scaling several-fold across California tree nuts and specialty. The thin-margin trap is real and named in our own research: the failure mode is selling a low-margin trade as if it were high-margin tech (Indigo, Silo). Our defense is that the early revenue is software-and-savings, not a logistics or lending balance sheet, and that the rebate audit in particular is high-leverage, find-once dollars with very low cost to serve. The gain-share aligns us with the grower and removes the "prove the ROI before I pay" objection entirely, which is the single biggest adoption blocker in this segment. The watch item is attribution: we need a clean, auditable method to prove a given dollar of savings is ours and not the grower's own dealer haggling. That is a product problem we have to solve before we bill anyone.

### Q: Why won't FBN or Nutrien just build this and crush us with their scale and capital?

A: Three structural reasons, in order of strength. First, conflict of interest. FBN, Nutrien, and the dealer channel are all sellers; an honest cross-supplier scout and a grower-side rebate audit work directly against their own margin. They will not build the tool that surfaces a competitor's lower price or claws back a rebate from a sale they made. Second, opacity is their business model. The rebate, zone-pricing, and prepay maze lets retailers price-discriminate by farm size and sophistication; dissolving it is value-destroying for them. Third, focus. FBN is row-crop and national-average centric and is currently contracting (2023 to 2025 layoffs, exited international, dropped seed, livestock, and fertilizer lines), and it raised $50M in July 2025 to expand AI, not to reinvent itself as an independent agent ([DigitalCommerce360](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2025/07/28/farmers-business-network-fbn-50-million-funding-ai/)). The honest counter is that none of this is a technology moat. They could copy the features. What they cannot copy without breaking their own P&L is the independent, non-seller position. Our real moat is execution plus that independence plus Terra's shared data model, the fact that we already understand this grower's ranches, entities, crops, and energy, which is what lets us derive the input bill of materials from a farm we already know. That is a head start, not a wall, and we have to keep running.

### Q: Data access. We need invoices, dealer accounts, and the crop plan. Farmers distrust handing receipts to a VC-backed third party. How do we get the data?

A: We lower the bar to the floor and we are radically clear about use. Onboarding is one step: forward or photo the invoices, which we read with vision the same way the energy tool reads bill PDFs. We do not require a dealer login. We never store financial or utility credentials anywhere the system or our team can read, a discipline carried straight from Tool 1. And we frame the data use honestly: your numbers do your work and feed an anonymized band that helps you, never shown with your name, never sold. Data distrust was a documented FBN complaint, and the trust position is precisely where we win, so we treat transparency about data as a feature, not compliance overhead.

### Q: What is the hardest thing to build, and what do we not know how to build yet?

A: Three hard problems. First, normalization: turning messy, inconsistent dealer invoices and a crop plan into a clean per-unit, same-active-ingredient comparison is the core legibility engine and it is genuinely hard at the SKU and active-ingredient level. We do know how to build it; it is labor, not mystery. Second, the rebate audit: manufacturer programs are idiosyncratic, tiered, and not machine-readable, so reconciling them against invoices is a modeling and data-entry challenge that may need a human-in-the-loop assist early. Third, the live-quote and PO rails: the agentic commerce standards are arriving (Agentic Commerce Protocol, Visa and Mastercard agent payments, Ever.Ag's freight agent), so we ride them rather than build them, but supplier catalogs are not machine-readable today, which is the real blocker on live scouting and the reason scouting is the second layer, not the first. The unknown we most need to retire early is savings attribution, named above.

### Q: Why us, why now, and what does this cannibalize?

A: Why us: same customer, same data model, same legibility brand as the energy tool, and the only player positioned to derive input demand from a farm it already understands. Why now: margins are negative so willingness to act on savings is at a peak (net farm income down about 22 percent from 2022 to 2024 ([AFBF](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/declining-farm-economy-continues-to-pressure-profitability))), working capital is strained so rebate and timing dollars matter more (2025 operating loans about 30 percent larger year over year, Chapter 12 bankruptcies up 46 percent ([KC Fed](https://www.kansascityfed.org/agriculture/agfinance-updates/larger-operating-loans-boost-farm-lending-activity-in-2025/))), the agentic commerce rails are now buildable, FBN is contracting out of the specialty lane, and a bipartisan Fertilizer Transparency Act would validate the thesis at the policy level ([Agri-Pulse](https://www.agri-pulse.com/articles/24432-house-bill-aims-to-provide-fertilizer-price-transparency)). It cannibalizes nothing; it is the natural Tool 2, a larger adjacent wallet sold to a customer who already trusts us. The cost is focus: doing this means not chasing Midwest row crops, where we have no distribution and no edge, and not building balance-sheet-heavy lending or logistics on day one.

### Q: What kills this?

A: The most likely killers, ranked. One, the channel coordinates to deny quotes and the live-scouting layer never reaches density, leaving us with only the audit-and-band value (survivable, but a smaller business than the pitch). Two, we cannot prove attribution cleanly, so gain-share billing becomes a fight with every customer and erodes the trust we sell on. Three, growers simply will not delegate, even one-tap, and the product stalls as a read-only dashboard nobody acts on (mitigated by leading with retrospective dollars, but real). Four, we lose discipline and chase the thin-margin trade as if it were high-margin tech, the exact failure mode that took down Indigo and Silo. None of these is fatal if we sequence correctly: legibility and rebate audit first (dollars with no dependencies), scouting and one-tap second (as density and trust build), delegation last (earned).

## The Verdict

This concept is worth building, and the discipline that makes it worth building is already written into the research: lead with legibility and the rebate audit, treat scouting and delegation as earned later layers, and stay capital-disciplined. On that posture, this is a strong Tool 2.

**Forged in steel.** The independent, non-seller position is the real asset, and it is genuinely defensible because the incumbents cannot copy it without breaking their own P&L (conflict of interest, opacity as business model, row-crop focus). The gain-share model is the right answer to the segment's single biggest objection, "prove the ROI before I pay," because it removes the ask entirely. The Terra fit is exceptional: same customer, same data model, same legibility brand, and a genuine head start in deriving the input bill of materials from a farm Terra already understands. The day-one value (legibility plus grower-side rebate audit) produces hard dollars with zero supplier dependency, which de-risks the launch.

**Needs more heat.** Savings attribution is the load-bearing unknown. Gain-share only works if we can prove, cleanly and auditably, that a given saved dollar is ours. That method has to be designed before we bill anyone, and it is not yet specified. The live-quote network's path to density in California specialty is asserted but not proven; we need a concrete plan for how many local dealers per quoting event makes the marketplace feel competitive, and where we expect that to fail. The rebate-audit engine's reliance on idiosyncratic, non-machine-readable programs likely needs a human-in-the-loop model early, and the cost-to-serve of that is not yet sized.

**Cracks in the foundation.** Channel retaliation is a genuine risk, not a hypothetical; FBN lived it. Our non-seller framing is a strong answer but not a guarantee, and we should expect some dealers to refuse to quote. Trust-to-delegate is the steepest climb in the whole thesis, and the only data we have is cross-industry and soft. The mitigation (retrospective dollars first, delegation last) is sound, but it means the agentic promise in the name is a destination, not a launch feature, and we must be honest internally that v1 is a legibility and audit tool with a one-tap PO, not an autonomous buyer. Finally, every savings figure in this market is vendor self-reported; our credibility depends on building our own provable, retrospective number from real invoices before we make a single claim. If we do that, the independence and the dollars carry the rest.
