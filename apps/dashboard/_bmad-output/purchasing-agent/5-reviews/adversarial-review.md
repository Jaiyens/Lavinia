# Adversarial Review: Terra Purchasing Agent

A cynical VC plus principal engineer teardown of the whole package: the [clone target and AI thesis](../0-research/clone-target-and-ai-thesis.md), [product brief](../1-analysis/product-brief.md), [PRFAQ](../1-analysis/prfaq.md), [PRD](../2-planning/prd.md), [epics](../2-planning/epics.md), and [architecture](../3-solutioning/architecture.md). The job here is to attack the weakest points, separate fatal objections from solvable ones, give the strongest steelman rebuttal for each, and name the evidence that would settle it. Plain operator English, no decoration.

## The one-paragraph verdict

This is the most honest BMAD package I have reviewed. It pre-empts most of the obvious attacks, demotes its own headline (the name says "purchasing agent" but v1 buys nothing), and the architecture openly contradicts the PRD on the single biggest technical lie (the Crop Plan does not exist in the schema). That honesty is real and it is load-bearing, but it is also a tell: the package has talked itself into a v1 that is so safe it may not be the business the thesis is selling. v1 is a single-grower invoice legibility plus rebate-audit tool for almond growers, free, with no marketplace, no negotiation, no pooled benchmark, and a band computed only from the grower's own invoices. That is buildable. Whether it is a venture-scale wedge, or a feature, is the question the package does not fully answer, and the gap between the buildable v1 and the defensible business is where the risk lives. The buildability verdict is go-with-conditions: the code can be built on the existing Terra stack, but one hard blocker (the Crop Plan) and several load-bearing unknowns must be closed before the epics that depend on them start.

## Fatal objections (these can kill the business, not just the build)

### F1. The v1 Market Band is single-grower, which quietly guts the whole transparency thesis

This is the deepest crack and the package half-hides it. The entire clone-and-beat logic is "FBN proved that showing a farmer what their neighbor paid makes them switch." That is a cross-grower comparison by definition. But v1's Market Band is computed only from this grower's own normalized invoices ([PRD FR-6](../2-planning/prd.md), [architecture band.ts](../3-solutioning/architecture.md)). A band built from one grower's own purchase history cannot tell that grower they overpaid versus the market. It can only tell them they paid more this time than last time, or more at dealer A than dealer B, for the same active ingredient. That is real but it is a much smaller claim than the thesis, and for a grower who buys one product from one dealer once a season (common in specialty), there is no band at all ("no reliable band yet"). The trojan-horse feature that the whole adoption story rests on is structurally weakest exactly at launch, when there is no network.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** The package is honest that pooling is POST-MVP and that thin data yields "no reliable band yet" rather than a fabricated number, which is more disciplined than FBN's own soft figures. The day-one dollars come from the rebate audit (which needs no band) and from same-active-ingredient and generic spreads visible within a single grower's own invoices (the grower buying the same chemistry at two prices, or buying branded when a generic existed). Those are real and need no network.
- **What would settle it:** Run the band engine against the real Batth export. Measure what fraction of a real grower's lines get a "reliable" single-grower band, and what dollar value those bands surface, versus the dollar value from the rebate audit and the generic-swap spread alone. If single-grower bands cover under, say, a third of spend with reliable bands, then the band is a marketing prop in v1 and the brief should stop leaning on the "you paid $X versus the market" framing until pooling ships. This is a one-week analysis on data Terra claims to have.

### F2. Gain-share is the entire revenue model and it rests on an unsolved problem the package admits it has not solved

The monetization is gain-share, and the package repeatedly and correctly names the prerequisite: Savings Attribution, proving a saved dollar is the agent's doing and not the grower's own haggling ([brief Vision](../1-analysis/product-brief.md), [PRD section 8 #1](../2-planning/prd.md), [PRFAQ internal FAQ](../1-analysis/prfaq.md)). It then says v1 is free precisely because attribution is unsolved. So the revenue model is parked behind a problem nobody has a method for. Worse, the architecture concedes that only under-credited rebate recovery is cleanly attributable (the agent found a missed credit); overpayment-versus-band is explicitly not attributable until the method is solved ([architecture section 8.5](../3-solutioning/architecture.md)). That collapses the billable surface of v1's headline feature (the band) to zero. The business that can actually bill on day-two is "rebate-recovery contingency fee," which is a real business but a far narrower one than "the purchasing agent that buys your inputs for less."

- **Steelman rebuttal:** Rebate recovery alone is genuinely attributable, high-leverage, find-once, low-cost-to-serve, and a defensible contingency-fee business on its own (this is the shape of medical-billing-audit and R&D-tax-credit firms, which are good businesses). The free v1 is explicitly the instrument that builds the invoice-level, loop-closed track record that makes broader attribution possible later. Naming attribution as a gate rather than hand-waving it is exactly right.
- **What would settle it:** Design one concrete attribution method and pressure-test it on real closed loops. Candidates: (a) attribute only the delta between the dealer's first quote and the final price after the agent surfaced the band, captured as a before/after artifact; (b) attribute only rebate recoveries, which are binary (credited or not). If neither produces a clean, defensible artifact a skeptical grower will accept, gain-share on anything but rebates is not billable, and the financial model in the internal FAQ ($5 to $20/acre revenue) is built on sand. The package should state the day-one billable business as "rebate-recovery contingency" and treat band-savings gain-share as a later, conditional revenue line.

### F3. The supplier-liquidity dodge is honest but it permanently caps the business at "dashboard plus audit"

The package decouples value from the marketplace: v1 produces dollars with zero suppliers because it runs on the grower's own invoices ([PRFAQ internal FAQ](../1-analysis/prfaq.md), [PRD risk table](../2-planning/prd.md)). Smart. But read it cynically: that decoupling is also an admission that the marketplace (scouting, negotiation, the actual agentic buying the name promises) may never reach density in California specialty, and the package half-concedes it ("the honest risk is that some regions or some product lines never reach quoting density. We accept that"). If scouting never lands, what remains is a single-grower spend dashboard plus a rebate auditor. That is the thin-margin trap wearing a different hat: not a thin-margin trade dressed as tech, but a thin-feature audit dressed as an agent. The "what kills this" section lists exactly this as killer #1 and rates it "survivable, but a smaller business than the pitch." A smaller business than the pitch is the definition of a thesis that does not clear the venture bar.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** Terra is not a standalone venture raising on this thesis; it is Tool 2 of an operating system whose distribution and customer are already paid for by Tool 1. A "dashboard plus audit" that lands on a warm customer at near-zero marginal CAC, on a shared data model, is a strong adjacent-wallet expansion even if it never becomes a marketplace. The bar for Tool 2 is "does it deepen the account and produce dollars," not "is it a standalone unicorn."
- **What would settle it:** Pick one almond-dense county and count it. How many independent dealers, co-ops, and direct/generic channels would actually quote a Batth-scale grower for a given active ingredient. If the answer is two or three for most chemistries, scouting can create real competition; if it is one (the grower's existing dealer, take it or leave it), the marketplace leg is dead and the package should stop selling it in the name and the PRFAQ headline. This is field research, not analysis, and it is the single most important unproven external fact in the whole package.

## Solvable objections (real risks with credible mitigations, evidence needed)

### S1. The Crop Plan does not exist, and the package shipped a PRD that says it does

The architecture is brutally honest here and deserves credit: section 4.6 and open-question #7 flatly contradict the PRD and brief, which both assert the Crop Plan is "already modeled by Terra for energy in Tool 1" and can be "consumed, not extended." It is not. The real `Crop` is `{ id, name, cropCoefficient, blocks, ranches, pumps }`; `Ranch`/`Block` carry acreage plus a nullable `cropId`; there is no program and no growth-stage schedule anywhere (verified against `prisma/schema.prisma`). The forecast formula "acres x crop x program x growth stage" has two of four factors that do not exist. Epic 3 (FR-3, FR-4) is blocked. This is a documentation-integrity failure in the upstream docs (the PRD repeats a false premise as load-bearing fact) caught only by the architect.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** This is exactly what the architecture phase is for, and it did its job: it found the lie, named it a blocker, and scoped two real resolutions (model a net-new `CropProgram` with a grower-supplied ingestion path, or scope FR-3/FR-4 down to a repeat-buy projection from prior invoices). Recommending path (b) first is the right call: it unblocks the calendar on data that exists.
- **What would settle it:** Make the decision before Epic 3 and fix the PRD. The PRD's open-question #7 ("how complete must the Crop Plan be") is the wrong question and the architecture says so; correct it to "the Crop Plan does not exist and must be modeled or scoped down." Until then, every cross-tool-leverage claim in the thesis ("Terra is the only player positioned to derive input demand from a farm it already understands") is unearned, because the farm Terra understands has no input program in it.

### S2. The "we are not a seller so the channel cannot retaliate" defense is asserted harder than the evidence supports

The independence position is the named moat. The package leans on it everywhere: incumbents cannot copy it without breaking their P&L. But the retaliation mechanism the package itself describes does not require the channel to see Terra as a rival seller. Dealers price-discriminate by farm size and sophistication; making that legible destroys margin whether or not Terra sells anything. A dealer who sees a grower arrive with a band-annotated order sheet and a rebate-audit claim has every incentive to slow-walk, decline to quote, or quietly reprice the relationship, exactly as if FBN had walked in. "We are not a seller" protects Terra from an antitrust-flavored fight, but it does not protect the grower's relationship, and the grower's relationship is the thing the package says is sticky (salesperson rated above brand). The non-seller framing answers the wrong threat.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** The mitigation is sequencing and the two day-one capabilities (own-invoice legibility and rebate audit) need zero dealer cooperation, so retaliation has nothing to bite on at launch. And "arm the grower for a better conversation, the dealer often matches the number" is a plausible equilibrium: a dealer who matches keeps the customer and the volume. The package is honest that some dealers will refuse and the grower loses nothing.
- **What would settle it:** Talk to three real almond growers and ask the uncomfortable question directly: if you showed your dealer a Terra-flagged order sheet, what happens to the relationship. The whole arm-the-advisor thesis (PRD UJ-4, Epic 7) is an untested behavioral bet. If even one grower says "my dealer would be insulted and I would never do that," the social-cost model is wrong and the product needs a softer surfacing than "here are three lines you overpaid on."

### S3. Trust-to-delegate is named the steepest climb, then the product avoids the climb entirely

The package repeatedly cites the steep climb (only ~27% trust autonomous AI for financial transactions, 53% of farmers need ROI proof) and then resolves it by making v1 transact nothing. That is honest, but it means the agentic promise in the name is deferred to "later, earned," and the only thing being validated in v1 is whether a grower will read a dashboard. The risk the package does not fully sit with: a read-only dashboard that recommends but never acts may simply not get used a second time. The "what kills this" section lists this as killer #3 ("growers will not delegate, even one-tap, and the product stalls as a read-only dashboard nobody acts on") and calls it "real." It is real, and the entire v1 is on the wrong side of it.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** Tool 1 is the existence proof. The same skeptical grower already uses Terra's read-only energy legibility, so a read-only input legibility tool on the same shell is a small ask, not a cold one. And the rebate audit produces a recover-this-now dollar action that is concrete enough to drive a second session.
- **What would settle it:** Measure second-session return on real onboarded growers, not consumer-trust survey proxies. The activation metric (SM-3, same-day first finding) is necessary but not sufficient; the metric that matters is whether the grower comes back and acts. Add a retention/return-and-act metric to the success criteria; its absence is a hole in section 7.

### S4. The rebate audit, the highest-value v1 feature, depends on data that is admittedly not machine-readable

Rebate recovery is the only cleanly attributable, day-one-billable feature (see F2), which makes it the most important thing in v1. But the package concedes repeatedly that manufacturer programs are idiosyncratic, tiered, and not machine-readable, so the audit leans on the internal Review queue and a human-in-the-loop assist, whose cost-to-serve is unsized ([PRD section 8 #4](../2-planning/prd.md), [PRFAQ internal FAQ](../1-analysis/prfaq.md)). So the crown-jewel feature is partly manual, at unknown unit cost, on a contingency-fee revenue model. If a human has to read each program's terms and reconcile by hand, the "high-leverage, find-once, low-cost-to-serve" claim is an assumption, not a fact, and the gain-share unit economics could invert.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** Rebate terms are stable within a season and reused across many growers and many invoices, so the human cost is amortized: read a program once, reconcile it against every grower who used it. The architecture routes ambiguity to the Review queue rather than asserting a number, so the failure mode is "slower," not "wrong."
- **What would settle it:** Get five real manufacturer rebate program sheets (almond chemistries) and time how long it takes a human to encode one into the `RebateProgram`/`RebateTier` schema, and how many growers a single encoded program serves. That gives the cost-to-serve numerator and denominator the package admits it does not have. Do this before promising any gain-share economics.

### S5. Onboarding requires both historical invoices and a usable crop plan, and the package excludes the growers who lack them

[PRD section 2.2](../2-planning/prd.md) names a non-user: "Growers with zero historical invoices and no crop plan." Combined with S1 (the crop plan does not exist as data anyway) and the invoice-by-photo onboarding, the real onboarding ask is "photograph or forward a full season of dealer invoices across six entities and four dealers." For a low-software-literacy grower in a truck, that is not the one-step setup the PRFAQ sells; it is a paperwork project. The package's own "lower the bar to the floor" framing undersells the actual friction of assembling a season of fragmented paper.

- **Steelman rebuttal:** Email-forward ingestion and the email-inbound webhook ([architecture section 9.1](../3-solutioning/architecture.md)) mean a grower can forward dealer emails as they arrive rather than assembling a stack, and the value compounds as more invoices land. The first finding can surface from a single invoice, so the grower sees value before they have uploaded everything.
- **What would settle it:** Watch one real grower attempt onboarding end to end and measure how many invoices they can actually produce in the first sitting, and whether the first finding lands before they give up. The same-day-finding target (SM-3) is testable but the package has not tested the upstream step: can the grower get enough paper into the system to make any finding at all.

## Buildability findings (the architecture-specific teardown)

The architecture is unusually strong and self-critical, and its load-bearing technical claims verify against the real repo. I checked: `result.ts` is genuinely PG&E-specialized (`firstPostedBillAfter` matches on `BillingPeriod.printedTotalCents`), `run-rate-lever.ts` really is delete-pending-and-recreate, the `src/lib/extract` ingestion pipeline exists to mirror, and the `Crop`/`Ranch`/`Account` schema shapes are exactly as the architecture describes. The self-criticism is accurate, not performative. Remaining buildability concerns:

- **B1. The Crop Plan blocker (see S1)** is correctly flagged as the one hard blocker and READY-EXCEPT status is the right call. Do not let Epic 3 start before the section 4.6 decision lands.
- **B2. The Finding immutability vs re-run idempotency tension is resolved on paper but is the single most error-prone implementation point.** The architecture is right that `run-rate-lever.ts`'s delete-pending-and-recreate would destroy the `lineageJson` that gain-share attribution depends on, and it specifies upsert-by-natural-key with a lineage-preservation DB test ([section 8.5](../3-solutioning/architecture.md), [section 13](../3-solutioning/architecture.md)). Good. But "upsert by lineage natural key, never overwrite, a changed input is a NEW finding" is subtle enough that a dev following the established `run-rate-lever` pattern by muscle memory will get it wrong. The lineage-preservation test must be written first, before `run-procurement.ts`, or it will be written to pass whatever the code happens to do.
- **B3. The shared `ActiveIngredient`/`Product` catalog is an unsized data dependency.** Same-active-ingredient normalization (FR-5) and generic-equivalent surfacing (FR-7) both require a populated catalog mapping SKUs to active ingredients to generics. The architecture flags catalog bootstrap as an open question (#6) and notes it must be sourced "without a paid third-party dataset, to respect the single-grower-band discipline." That constraint is self-imposed and possibly self-defeating: building an almond-chemistry catalog by hand is real labor, and CDPR/EPA label data is public and would accelerate it. The normalization engine is useless without the catalog, so this is a hidden critical-path dependency hiding inside an "open question."
- **B4. Idempotent re-ingest by `sourceHash` plus invoice identity is sound, but photo re-ingest is the soft spot.** Two photos of the same invoice produce different bytes and different `sourceHash`, so the design correctly falls back to the extracted `(dealerId, invoiceNumber, invoiceDate)` identity. But that identity comes from a vision extraction that the design itself says is sometimes low-confidence and routed to review. A grower who photographs the same invoice twice, both slightly blurry, could create two invoice rows that never dedup. This is a known-hard problem and the architecture half-addresses it; it needs an explicit test for the blurry-duplicate case.
- **B5. No NFR or test addresses cost or latency of vision extraction at Batth scale.** 180-plus invoice lines across a season, read by Opus-default vision with a cheaper-first escalation lever, is a real per-onboarding LLM cost on a free product. The architecture says ingestion is "latency-tolerant" and "cheap CPU" for calc, but the vision read is neither cheap nor free, and on a gain-share-someday, free-today model the per-onboarding extraction cost is an unmodeled COGS line. Size it.

## Smaller issues and inconsistencies worth fixing

- **I1. The name oversells v1 and the package knows it.** "Purchasing Agent" and the PRFAQ headline ("the buyer that works for your farm") describe a product that buys. v1 buys nothing. [PRD section 6.2](../2-planning/prd.md) even carries a NOTE FOR PM to keep internal messaging honest that v1 is "legibility plus audit, not an autonomous buyer." If the internal docs need a standing reminder not to believe their own name, the name is a liability. Consider whether v1 should launch under a more honest sub-name (the legibility-and-audit tool) and reserve "Agent" for when it acts.
- **I2. The per-acre revenue math is a chain of estimates presented with suspicious precision.** "$5 to $20/acre revenue... across 100,000 to 200,000 acres... $0.5M to $4M early annual revenue" ([PRFAQ internal FAQ](../1-analysis/prfaq.md)) multiplies an estimated capture rate by an estimated savings share by an estimated acreage by an estimated penetration. Every factor is labeled estimate, which is honest, but the product of four estimates spanning 4x ranges each is not a number, it is a hope. The range understates its own uncertainty.
- **I3. Every external savings figure in the package is vendor-self-reported and the package says so, repeatedly.** The 15%, 283%, 468%, 35% generic, ~25% rebate, 40% energy figures are all FBN/CropLife/Wexus self-reported and unaudited. The package's own answer ("which is exactly why we build our own provable number") is correct, but it means the entire why-now case rests on numbers the package itself does not trust. The honest move is already made; just recognize the thesis has no independently verified savings figure anywhere under it.
- **I4. The PCA-as-ally bet is asserted, never evidenced.** The PCA is the legal gatekeeper and is in the trusted circle ~100% of the time, and v1 arms them with read-only visibility. But a PCA is usually employed by or affiliated with the dealer whose prices the agent is flagging. "Arm the advisor" assumes the advisor's interests align with the grower's on price; for a dealer-employed PCA they may not. PRD open-question #5 flags pressure-testing PCA reception, which is right, but the package presents the arm-the-advisor posture as a strength before testing whether the advisor will cooperate against their own employer's margin.
- **I5. "Specialty-crop fit" is asserted as a moat but v1 contains almost nothing almond-specific.** The differentiation claim is "row-crop incumbents are weak for permanent crops." But the v1 engine (ingest, normalize, band, rebate audit, spend table) is crop-agnostic; nothing in the FRs or architecture is almond-specific except the catalog seed and the cost-study references. The specialty moat is distribution (Terra's existing CA base) plus a chemistry catalog, not product architecture. That is fine, but the brief frames it as a product-fit advantage it has not built.
- **I6. The success metrics have no return/retention metric.** SM-1 through SM-6 measure identified savings, accuracy, activation, loop closure, coverage, and advisor engagement. None measures whether the grower comes back and acts a second time, which (per S3) is the actual existential question for a read-only v1. Add it.
- **I7. Data-retention window and deletion SLA are explicitly unresolved** ([PRD Data Governance](../2-planning/prd.md), NOTE FOR PM). For a tool holding a grower's full financial invoice history on a trust-first positioning, an undefined retention/deletion policy is a launch-blocker for the trust story, not a detail.

## What I would demand before greenlighting the build

1. **Settle the Crop Plan decision (S1, B1)** and fix the PRD's false "already modeled" premise. Recommend path (b), repeat-buy projection, for v1. Hard gate on Epic 3.
2. **Run the single-grower band against the real Batth export (F1)** and report what fraction of spend gets a reliable band and what dollars it surfaces versus rebate-audit dollars. This decides whether the band is a v1 feature or v1 vaporware.
3. **Design one concrete, defensible Savings Attribution artifact (F2)** even if it only covers rebate recovery, and state the day-one billable business as rebate-recovery contingency, not band-savings gain-share.
4. **Count the dealers in one almond county (F3, S2).** This is the cheapest, highest-information piece of evidence in the whole package and it decides whether the marketplace leg is alive.
5. **Time the human cost of encoding one real rebate program (S4)** and size the per-onboarding vision-extraction cost (B5) so the gain-share economics rest on a measured COGS, not an assumed one.
6. **Add a return-and-act retention metric (S3, I6)** and resolve the retention/deletion policy (I7) before launch.

None of these is a reason not to build the v1 engine. The pure-calc core, the ingestion pipeline, the recommendation grammar, and the views all extend proven Tool 1 patterns and are genuinely low-risk to construct. The risk is not "can we build it." The risk is "after we build it, is it the wedge the thesis promised, or a competent feature on a warm customer that never becomes the agent in the name." The package is honest enough to have already written that sentence itself, in the "what kills this" section. The conditions above are how you find out before you have spent the build.
