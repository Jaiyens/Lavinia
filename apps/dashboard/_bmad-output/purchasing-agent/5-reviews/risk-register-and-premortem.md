# Risk Register and Pre-Mortem: Terra Purchasing Agent

## How to read this

This is a pre-mortem, not a retrospective. Nothing is built. We are standing 18 months in the future, the Purchasing Agent has failed, and we are working backward to ask why. The posture is the same as a retrospective: no blame, focus on systems, every finding tied to a concrete leading indicator and a mitigation with an owner. The difference is that we get to act on these before they happen instead of after.

The honest read on the source documents first, because it changes the shape of this register. The thesis, PRD, and architecture are unusually self-critical. They name almost every risk that matters and they label the soft numbers as soft. That is good discipline and it lowers the odds of a blind-side failure. But naming a risk is not retiring it. Most of the named risks are handled by deferral: the hard parts (negotiation, scouting, auto-buy, gain-share billing, cross-grower pooling, the Crop Plan) are pushed to POST-MVP or parked as Open Questions with no committed resolution date and no owner. A pre-mortem's job is to ask what happens if those deferred items never resolve, because "we will solve it later" is exactly the assumption that kills products 18 months out.

The single most important framing: v1 as specified is a free, single-grower, retrospective legibility-and-audit tool with no transaction, no network benchmark, and no billing. Every dollar of revenue, every competitive moat that depends on scale, and the headline of the product name ("buys your inputs for less") lives in POST-MVP. So the central pre-mortem question is not "can we build v1." We can, minus the Crop Plan. The question is "does v1 earn the right to build v2 before the money, the patience, or the trust runs out." That is where this fails.

## Pre-mortem failure narrative (the most likely obituary)

Eighteen months out, the story reads like this. v1 shipped on time and worked. Growers connected invoices, the Spend Table was genuinely the first legible view of their input spend, and they liked it. Then three things happened together. The single-grower Market Band turned out to be too thin to flag overpayment with confidence on most lines (a grower buys a given active ingredient from one or two dealers a couple times a season, which is not enough points for a credible band), so the headline "you overpaid by $X" finding fired rarely and the rebate audit became the only reliable money-finder. The rebate audit found real money, but only for a subset of growers in a subset of programs, and each one leaned on the unsized internal Review queue, so cost-to-serve crept up while the product stayed free. And because there was no billing and no clear path to billing (savings attribution was never solved, so gain-share never turned on), the product had a warm but small user base, a rising ops cost, and no revenue. Meanwhile the Crop Plan never got modeled, so the Buy Window Calendar shipped as a repeat-buy projection that growers found underwhelming, and the forward-looking half of the product that justified "agent" never arrived. The dealers noticed the rebate audits showing up in their conversations and quietly made life harder. Terra's attention went back to Tool 1 and the third tool. The Purchasing Agent was not killed in a meeting; it was starved. That is the most likely death: not a crash, a slow bleed from a free tool that could not prove it should be paid for.

The less likely but faster death: a savings number we asserted turned out wrong in front of a grower's PCA, the PCA called it amateur, word moved through a tight California grower network, and the trust the whole product runs on evaporated in one season.

## Prioritized risk register

Risks are ordered by a rough product of likelihood and impact on the project surviving to a billable v2. Likelihood and impact are High / Medium / Low. Each carries the leading indicator to watch (the thing that tells you it is happening before it is fatal) and a concrete mitigation with an owner.

### R1. Savings attribution is never solved, so gain-share never turns on and the product has no revenue model

- **Likelihood: High.** The PRD and architecture both flag this as the load-bearing unknown (PRD section 8 #1, architecture section 8.5 and 14.5 #4) and neither proposes a method, only the evidence trail that a method would need. "We will figure out attribution from the track record" is a hope, not a plan. Proving a saved dollar is the agent's doing and not the grower's own haggling is genuinely hard, possibly unsolvable cleanly for overpayment-vs-band (the grower could always have negotiated there anyway).
- **Impact: High.** This is the whole business model. No attribution means no honest gain-share, which means v1 is free and there is no specified path to charging for anything. A free tool with a real cost-to-serve and no billing is a charity, not a company.
- **Leading indicator:** Months pass with the attribution Open Question still open and no owner. Or: the gap between identified savings (SM-1) and attributed realized savings (SM-1b) stays wide and does not close as loops close (SM-C1 widening). The honest tell is that SM-1b stays tiny while SM-1 grows.
- **Mitigation:** Make attribution an owned, dated work item now, not a someday Open Question. Decide before the first paying conversation which finding types are cleanly attributable and bill only on those. The cleanest is under-credited rebate recovery (the agent found a missed credit the grower demonstrably did not have, a clean "but-for" case); make that the first and possibly only gain-share basis for a long time, and price it as a recovery fee on found-and-claimed rebate dollars, not as a share of fuzzy band savings. Accept that overpayment-vs-band may never be billable and design the revenue model so it does not have to be. **Owner: Jaiyen (business model), with the PM specifying the attributable-finding taxonomy.**

### R2. The single-grower Market Band is too thin to produce credible overpayment findings, so the trojan-horse feature misfires

- **Likelihood: High.** This is structural, not a calibration nuisance. The v1 band is computed only from one grower's own normalized invoices (FR-6), and a California almond grower buys a given active ingredient from a small number of dealers a small number of times per season. That is often too few comparable points for a credible low/median/high. The architecture's own honesty gate ("no reliable band yet") will fire constantly on exactly the lines growers most want flagged, and cross-grower pooling that would fix it is explicitly POST-MVP and needs network density that does not exist on day one.
- **Impact: High.** The price-transparency wedge is the trojan horse, the feature FBN proved farmers love. If overpayment flags rarely fire (because most bands are "no reliable band yet"), v1 collapses to "a nice spend table plus a rebate audit." That may not be enough to earn the next tool, and it strands the single most-cited reason this thesis exists.
- **Leading indicator:** On the first real Batth-shaped invoice set, count what fraction of active ingredients reach "reliable" vs "no reliable band yet." If most lines cannot get a band, the wedge is misfiring. Also watch the SM-1 overpayment contribution vs the rebate contribution: if overpayment is near zero, the band is thin.
- **Mitigation:** Validate the band on real Batth invoices before committing to the single-grower constraint as a launch posture (PRD Open Question #3). If single-grower bands are too thin (likely), either (a) accept that v1's money story is rebate-audit-led and overpayment is secondary, and message it that way internally and externally so nobody oversells the band, or (b) bring a non-network reference price in carefully (a public/list-price anchor or a regional reference that is disclosed as such, not presented as a network benchmark) so a band exists even with thin own-data, while keeping cross-grower pooling for later. Do not ship a product whose headline finding almost never fires. **Owner: PM (validation on real invoices), Architect (band.ts reliability config and any reference-price source decision).**

### R3. The Crop Plan does not exist in the data model, blocking the entire forecast and calendar cluster

- **Likelihood: High (it is already true).** The architecture is blunt: the "Crop Plan" the PRD assumes is "already modeled by Terra for energy" does not exist (architecture section 4.6, 14.5 #7). The energy `Crop` has no program and no growth-stage schedule, so the forecast formula has nothing to read. FR-3, FR-4, and Epic 3 are blocked.
- **Impact: High.** The Buy Window Calendar is the home screen, the graspable-in-seconds hook, and the forward-looking half that justifies the word "agent." Without a real Crop Plan, the calendar ships as a repeat-buy projection (path b), which is honest but materially weaker and undercuts the "plan what you will need before the season" job-to-be-done.
- **Leading indicator:** Epic 3 approaches with the section 4.6 decision still unmade. Or path (b) ships and grower feedback on the calendar is flat ("this just tells me what I already bought").
- **Mitigation:** Force the section 4.6 decision before Epic 3 starts, with an owner. Ship path (b) repeat-buy projection first to unblock the calendar on real data, clearly labeled as a projection from prior purchases, not a fabricated agronomic plan. In parallel, scope path (a) net-new `CropProgram` plus a grower-supplied ingestion path as the route to the real forecast, and be honest in the PRD that the agronomic Crop Plan is net-new work and a new onboarding step, not a free reuse from Tool 1. Correct the PRD language that calls the Crop Plan "already modeled." **Owner: Architect (model decision), PM (PRD correction and the new onboarding step).**

### R4. Channel conflict with dealers and co-ops turns the trusted-advisor circle against the product

- **Likelihood: Medium.** v1 is defensively designed against this: it is not a seller, requires no dealer login, and leads with the grower's own invoices and rebate audit, which need no dealer cooperation. That genuinely lowers day-one risk. But the rebate audit is, in effect, telling the grower their dealer shorted them a credit, and a grower waving that in a dealer conversation is adversarial whether or not Terra calls itself the grower's agent. FBN faced exactly this hostility (including a competition probe). The Big Seven control ~70% of inputs and the relationship is the switching cost.
- **Impact: High.** The PCA and dealer rep are in the trusted-advisor circle nearly 100% of the time. If they decide the agent is an enemy, they can poison it to the grower in one conversation, and the product loses the relationship it was built to respect. In a tight California specialty-grower network, that travels fast.
- **Leading indicator:** PCA/dealer reactions in the first pilot conversations (PRD Open Question #5). Watch for advisors framing the tool as "that app that fights your dealer." Watch dealer responsiveness to flagged rebate claims (do they pay, or do they stall and badmouth).
- **Mitigation:** Hold the arm-the-advisor posture as hard product, not just brand. The PCA read-only visibility (FR-15/16) is the relationship insurance, ship it day one and make the PCA's confirm/dispute a real feedback channel so the advisor feels armed, not surveilled. Frame every rebate finding as "make sure you got what your program already owed you," which is a claim against the manufacturer program, not an accusation against the local rep. Pressure-test the framing with two or three real grower-PCA pairs before any broad launch. **Owner: PM (positioning and the pilot test), UX (the advisor-facing framing of findings).**

### R5. Trust to delegate spend never materializes, so the product stalls as a free viewer with no path to action or revenue

- **Likelihood: Medium-High.** Only ~27% of people trust autonomous AI for financial transactions (cross-industry), and 53% of NA farmers demand clear ROI before buying ag-tech. The product's answer is "earn trust by proving retrospective accuracy, then close the loop, then one-tap, then auto-buy, much later." That ladder is sound but every rung after legibility is POST-MVP. If trust accrues slowly (likely, with a skeptical Excel-line-by-line operator), the product can sit on the bottom rung indefinitely.
- **Impact: High.** The transition from "shows me money" to "I let it act / I pay it from savings" is where the business lives. A product that is loved but never trusted enough to transact or be billed is the slow-bleed death in the obituary above.
- **Leading indicator:** Retrospective accuracy (SM-2) and loop closure (SM-4) are strong but conversion to any paid or delegated action stays at zero quarter over quarter. Growers say "great tool" and never move past free.
- **Mitigation:** Instrument the trust ladder explicitly and treat stalling on a rung as a signal, not a phase. Get the close-the-loop predicted-vs-actual evidence (FR-12) in front of growers fast and repeatedly, because that is the trust-building artifact. Pick the one POST-MVP rung that needs the least trust and the most obvious value (rebate-claim assist, where the grower is recovering money already owed) as the first thing past legibility, rather than waiting for full negotiation/auto-buy. Do not let v1 be a feature-complete dead end. **Owner: Jaiyen (the v1-to-v1.5 trust-ladder plan), PM (loop-closure UX as the trust artifact).**

### R6. Cost-to-serve from the internal Review queue outruns a free product

- **Likelihood: Medium-High.** The Review queue is real v1 ops labor and its cost is explicitly unsized (PRD Open Question #4, architecture 14.5 #5). Three things route to it: blurry invoice lines, un-normalizable units, and ambiguous rebate terms. Manufacturer rebate programs are idiosyncratic and not machine-readable, so the rebate audit (the most valuable feature) leans on the queue most. The product is free in v1. Free product plus human-in-the-loop ops labor that scales with usage is a cost curve that bends the wrong way.
- **Impact: Medium-High.** Rising per-grower ops cost with zero revenue is a direct path to the project being starved. It also caps how many growers you can onboard before the queue becomes the bottleneck.
- **Leading indicator:** Review-queue depth and the `needs_review` / `possible-needs-confirmation` rate per onboarded farm (architecture section 12 names these as the signals). If queue items per farm are not falling as the catalog and extraction improve, cost-to-serve is not converging.
- **Mitigation:** Size the queue cost-to-serve on the first cohort before scaling, with a hard cap on items-per-farm that triggers a review of the extraction threshold and catalog coverage. Invest early in the shared `ActiveIngredient` / `Product` catalog and the rebate-program templates so the same chemistry and the same common programs stop hitting the queue twice (catalog bootstrap, architecture 14.5 #6). Bound the queue and measure it from day one rather than discovering the cost at scale. **Owner: Architect (extraction threshold and catalog), Jaiyen (ops cost model and the cap).**

### R7. Supplier and network density never reaches the level the POST-MVP product needs

- **Likelihood: Medium.** v1 is deliberately decoupled from supplier density (legibility and rebate audit need zero suppliers in network), which is the right call and lowers near-term risk. But the headline product ("scouts everyone, buys for less") needs live cross-supplier quoting, and California specialty is thin outside the Midwest row-crop footprint FBN and GROWERS built around. The thing that justifies the name may never have enough participating dealers to function.
- **Impact: Medium.** It does not kill v1. It caps the ceiling: the product can be a permanent legibility-plus-audit tool and never become the buying agent the name promises, which limits the revenue model and the moat.
- **Leading indicator:** When scouting is attempted (POST-MVP), the count of dealers willing to quote in the target California specialty geography. If RFQs go out and few come back, density is not there.
- **Mitigation:** Keep the value decoupled from the marketplace as long as possible, exactly as the architecture does. Build live-quoting first where Terra already has grower density, so the network bootstraps off existing customers rather than cold supply. Be honest internally that v1's name oversells v1's function (the PRD already flags this in section 6.2), and do not let go-to-market promise scouting before density exists. **Owner: Jaiyen (geo sequencing of the scouting build), PM (honest internal/external messaging on what v1 does).**

### R8. Data access: growers will not hand a VC-style third party their invoices and crop plan

- **Likelihood: Medium.** The product runs entirely on the grower supplying their own invoices and (for the real forecast) their crop plan. Farmers distrust handing receipts and field data to a third party; this was a documented FBN complaint. The mitigation (no dealer login, photo/forward onboarding, clear data-use framing, the Tool 1 credential discipline) is strong, and the dual-tool grower already trusts Terra with energy, which helps.
- **Impact: Medium-High.** No invoices means no product. The whole retrospective engine is empty without the grower's documents.
- **Leading indicator:** Onboarding drop-off at the "forward your invoices" step. Activation (SM-3, time from first invoice to first finding) cannot start if the invoice never arrives.
- **Mitigation:** Lean on the existing Terra relationship: the energy-tool grower already trusted Terra with their PG&E data, so lead Tool 2 with that earned trust rather than a cold ask. Make the data-use framing radically clear (single-grower band, no pooling, no selling, exportable, deletable). Keep the onboarding to one low-friction step (forward or photo). Confirm the retention window and deletion SLA before launch (PRD Data Governance note). **Owner: PM (onboarding flow and data-use copy), UX (the trust framing at the connect step).**

### R9. Thin-margin trap: the product drifts toward balance-sheet-heavy logistics or lending and loses capital discipline

- **Likelihood: Low-Medium.** v1 is explicitly software-and-savings, no logistics, no lending, which is the correct lesson from Indigo and Silo. The risk is later: the POST-MVP roadmap includes attached crop-cycle financing and transaction spread, and the pull toward a money-moving rail (which the thesis correctly says durable winners have) can quietly become a balance-sheet business if not disciplined.
- **Impact: High (if it happens).** This is the failure mode that destroyed the comparable companies. A thin-margin trade dressed as high-margin tech burns capital and gets marked down ~90% (Indigo) or blows up (Silo).
- **Leading indicator:** Roadmap or fundraising conversations that lead with logistics or lending volume rather than software margin and gain-share. Any plan that puts Terra's balance sheet between the grower and the dealer.
- **Mitigation:** Hold the v1 discipline as a permanent rule: lead with software legibility and gain-share, treat any financing or transaction rail as opt-in and capital-light (spread or partnership, not Terra's own balance sheet) and only after the software value is proven. Keep the rebate audit, which is find-once and low-cost-to-serve, as the high-leverage core. **Owner: Jaiyen.**

### R10. Regulatory: CA DPR / FIFRA gatekeeping on crop-protection recommendations

- **Likelihood: Low (for v1 as scoped).** In California a licensed PCA is the legal gatekeeper for crop-protection recommendations, and FIFRA governs pesticide use. v1 does not recommend what to apply; it audits price and rebates on inputs the grower and PCA already chose, and it forecasts a bill of materials from the grower's own plan. So v1 is mostly outside the recommendation-licensing line. The risk rises sharply at POST-MVP when the Crop Plan forecast (path a) starts implying or generating a spray program, and when the agent moves toward suggesting product swaps (even a generic-equivalent swap touches "which product to buy").
- **Impact: Medium-High (if mischaracterized).** If a regulator or a hostile PCA frames the agent as making an unlicensed crop-protection recommendation, that is a compliance problem and a trust problem at once.
- **Leading indicator:** Any product copy or feature that crosses from "here is the price on what you and your PCA chose" into "you should apply this instead." The generic-equivalent surfacing (FR-7) is the closest edge in v1: surfacing a cheaper same-active-ingredient SKU is a price fact, but if it reads as "use this product," it drifts toward the PCA's licensed territory.
- **Mitigation:** Keep v1 strictly on the price/rebate/spend side of the line and keep the PCA in the loop by design (advisor visibility is also the compliance shield, not just a relationship move). Frame the generic equivalent as a price-comparison fact on the same active ingredient the PCA already specified, never as an agronomic recommendation. Before path (a) Crop Plan ingestion ships, get a clear read on where the BoM forecast sits relative to PCA licensing and FIFRA, and ensure any program suggestion is the PCA's, surfaced by the agent, not the agent's own. **Owner: Jaiyen (regulatory read before POST-MVP forecast), PM (copy discipline on the recommendation line).**

### R11. Key-person and executive concentration risk

- **Likelihood: Medium.** This is Tool 2 of a small operation; the artifacts show a tight set of owners (Jaiyen across business, model, and most cross-cutting calls). The load-bearing unsolved problems (savings attribution, the Crop Plan decision, band reliability, ops cost) are concentrated decisions that depend heavily on one or two people, and the grower relationships that make California specialty work are personal and few.
- **Impact: Medium-High.** If the key person's attention shifts (back to Tool 1, or to Tool 3, which the obituary above describes), the Purchasing Agent's open decisions stall and the product is starved rather than killed. Concentration also means a single departure or burnout takes the institutional context with it.
- **Leading indicator:** Open Questions sitting unowned and undated for long stretches. Decisions waiting on one person. The few founding grower relationships carried by a single team member with no backup.
- **Mitigation:** Assign each load-bearing Open Question a named owner and a decision date now (this register does that as a starting point). Document the grower and PCA relationships so they are not single-threaded. Sequence Terra's tools so the Purchasing Agent has dedicated attention through its trust-building window rather than competing with the next tool for the same person. **Owner: Jaiyen (owner assignment and tool sequencing).**

## The top 3 kill criteria

These are the conditions under which the honest move is to stop, not to push harder. A kill criterion is not a risk; it is a pre-committed line that, if crossed, means the core thesis was wrong for this product. Naming them now is how we avoid the sunk-cost version of the obituary, where the product limps for a year because no one would call it.

### Kill criterion 1: No clean, billable savings attribution exists after the v1 evidence is in

If, after v1 has run on a real cohort and closed loops for a full season, we still cannot define a finding type that is cleanly attributable to the agent and that a grower would pay a share of without arguing, then the gain-share business model is dead and there is no other specified way to charge for this. If rebate recovery alone (the one clean case) is too small a base to build a business on, and overpayment-vs-band cannot be honestly attributed, stop. A free tool with a real cost-to-serve and no billing path is not a product. **The test: by the end of the first full closed-loop season, is there at least one finding type generating attributed realized savings (SM-1b) large enough and clean enough to bill, with growers agreeing it was the agent's doing. If no, kill or fundamentally repivot the monetization.**

### Kill criterion 2: The single-grower band is structurally too thin AND no honest alternative reference exists

If validation on real Batth-shaped invoices shows that most active ingredients land in "no reliable band yet" (the structurally likely outcome), AND we cannot find an honest non-network reference price to anchor a band without misrepresenting it as a network benchmark, then the price-transparency wedge, the proven heart of the FBN thesis, does not function for a single California specialty grower. If the only fix is cross-grower pooling that requires a network we do not have and cannot bootstrap, the wedge is not available to us at the scale we operate. The product would reduce to a rebate audit plus a spend table, which may be a feature but is not the thesis. **The test: on real invoices, what share of a grower's active-ingredient lines can carry a credible band. If that share is too low to drive the overpayment finding AND no honest reference price closes the gap, the transparency wedge is not viable for v1 and the project should be reconsidered, not relaunched on hope of future network density.**

### Kill criterion 3: The trusted-advisor circle rejects the product in pilot

If, in the first real grower-PCA pilots, the PCAs and dealers consistently treat the agent as an adversary that damages the grower's relationship with their advisor, and the arm-the-advisor posture does not flip that, then the product is fighting the one structural fact this thesis built its entire mitigation around (the advisor is in the circle nearly 100% of the time). In a small, relationship-dense California specialty-grower world, an advisor circle that rejects the tool is not a marketing problem to out-message; it is the market telling you the wedge does not survive contact with the relationship. **The test: across the first handful of grower-PCA pilot pairs, do the PCAs engage with advisor visibility and confirm findings (SM-6 meaningful), or do they uniformly frame the tool as the app that fights your dealer. If the latter and the framing fixes do not move it, stop before burning the network, because the network does not forget.**

## Overall risk posture

Not ready to commit as a fundable v2 path without conditions, but the v1 build is sound and worth doing as the evidence-gathering step. The architecture and PRD are honest and buildable; the failure modes are not technical, they are business-model and trust-and-timing risks that the documents name but defer. The three kill criteria (attribution, band viability, advisor acceptance) are the questions v1 exists to answer, and they should be answered deliberately and early, not discovered at scale. Build v1 to learn, with the attribution and band questions instrumented from day one, and treat the kill criteria as real lines, not formalities.
