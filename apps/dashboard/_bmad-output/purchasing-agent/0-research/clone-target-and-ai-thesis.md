# Clone Target & AI Thesis

The fastest way to build something people want is to copy a model that already works, then make it materially better along an axis the incumbent structurally cannot follow. This is the Paul Graham / YC move: start from a proven shape, not a blank page, and win on the part the leader is too big, too conflicted, or too spread thin to fix. For Terra's second tool, the proven shape is input procurement, and the incumbent to copy is Farmers Business Network.

## The Decision

**We copy Farmers Business Network (FBN), specifically its input-procurement model, and we beat it with an AI-native, farmer-side purchasing agent built for California specialty growers.**

FBN is the right base for four reasons.

First, the wedge is proven. FBN's whole company is built on one insight: input pricing is opaque, and once you show a farmer what their neighbor paid for the same active ingredient, they get angry and they switch. Their own transparency reports document up to a ~15% spread between list and price actually paid, with claims of much wider variation (up to 283% on a single product, and up to 468% in their 2024 report) farmer to farmer ([FBN 2023 transparency report](https://markets.financialcontent.com/clarkebroadcasting.mycentraloregon/article/bizwire-2023-3-23-amidst-volatile-market-fbn-releases-2023-chemical-price-transparency-report), [FBN 2024 report via AgWeb](https://www.agweb.com/news/business/fbn-releases-its-2024-ag-chemical-price-transparency-report)). These savings figures are FBN-self-reported and not independently audited (estimate), and FBN's own data admits the below-average half of buyers got only ~14% off list, not the headline 50%. But even hedged, the wedge is real and it has been validated at scale.

Second, the prize is enormous and it is the dominant controllable cost. Crop inputs (chemicals, fertilizer, seed) were $72.2B, or 28.6% of crop-farm total expenses, in 2024 ([USDA NASS via AFBF](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/declining-farm-economy-continues-to-pressure-profitability)). For corn, seed + chemicals + fertilizer is ~73% of operating cost ([Southern Ag Today](https://southernagtoday.org/2025/07/07/recent-trends-in-farm-operating-costs/)). Note: $72.2B is USDA's share-of-crop-farm-expenses construct, not the simple sum of the all-farm line items (fertilizer/lime $33.8B + seeds $27.4B + ag chemicals $21.7B, which is ~$83B). Inputs are where the money is.

Third, the timing is forced. Input prices sit ~20-40% above pre-2021 levels (seed +18%, chemicals +25%, fuel +31%, fertilizer +37% since 2020) while commodity prices fell ~50% from 2022 peaks, pushing every major row crop to a negative 2025 per-acre margin (corn -$169, cotton -$379, soybeans -$114) ([AFBF](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/declining-farm-economy-continues-to-pressure-profitability)). In a negative-margin year, a few points off procurement is the difference between loss and breakeven. That is exactly when a savings tool gets adopted.

Fourth, the incumbent has structural gaps we can drive through. FBN spread itself thin (commerce + finance + insurance + carbon + seed) and is now contracting (2023-2025 layoffs, exited Australia and international, dropped seed/livestock/fertilizer lines). It is row-crop and national-average centric, which is weak for California specialty and permanent crops. And critically, it is a seller, not the farmer's independent agent, an inherent conflict of interest. Its AI is "Norm," an LLM advisor on OpenAI GPT-3.5 launched April 2023 for agronomy and crop-marketing Q&A ([AgFunder](https://agfundernews.com/why-farmers-business-network-launched-norm-an-ai-advisor-for-farmers-built-on-chatgpt)). To be precise and honest: FBN does use AI. What does not exist anywhere is an autonomous, farmer-side purchasing agent that forecasts demand, scouts prices across suppliers, audits rebates, negotiates, and prepares the PO. That is the white space.

## What We Copy

The proven, working parts of FBN's model that we adopt directly:

- **Crowdsourced price transparency as the trojan horse.** Aggregate anonymized invoice/price data across members, normalize it to a per-unit, same-active-ingredient comparison, and show each grower "you paid $X versus the network median." This is the single feature farmers love and even non-buyers use to negotiate with their existing dealer. It is the legibility-first move, the same one Terra's energy tool makes for PG&E bills.
- **Direct-from-manufacturer / cut-the-markup sourcing,** including flagging the cheaper same-active-ingredient generic equivalent (FBN claims up to ~35% on a brand-to-generic swap; FBN-sourced, directional (estimate)).
- **Free membership, monetize the transaction.** FBN dropped its $700/yr fee to $0 in 2020 to grow the network and now monetizes via commerce, financing spread, and private-label margin. Subscription is not the business; the wallet is.
- **Reliable direct-to-farm delivery as the trust-builder.** FBN's most-cited five-star reason is "they deliver when they say they will." Logistics reliability is a feature, not an afterthought.
- **Attached financing tied to the crop cycle.** Inputs are bought up front and recouped only at harvest, so working capital is the input problem. FBN passed $1B in operating lines and reports $3B total financing extended (FBN-reported milestones, not independently verified (estimate)). The lesson ports: a money-moving rail attached to the data is what every durable ag-tech winner has, and pure-software farm-ops layers that lacked one (Granular, Conservis, Agrible) all got absorbed by strategics.

## How We Beat It With AI

FBN proved the wedge but stopped at chat-plus-benchmarks. We build the agent FBN has not: a farmer-side autonomous purchasing agent. Each capability below is something the agent does, and each maps to a gap incumbents structurally cannot or will not close.

**1. Autonomous demand forecasting from the crop and irrigation plan.** The agent translates acres x crop x program x growth stage into a per-product bill of materials and a buying calendar, automatically. No incumbent does this today; price comparison is retrospective ("what others paid"), never a forward plan. Because Terra already models the farm's blocks, crops, and (via Tool 1) its irrigation/pump energy, we can derive the input BoM from data the grower already gave us. FBN cannot match this because it has no crop-plan model; it is a catalog, not a planner.

**2. Cross-supplier price scouting and live quoting.** FBN benchmarks historical paid prices inside its walled garden. Our agent actively scouts live quotes across multiple local dealers, co-ops, and direct/generic channels, then normalizes them to per-unit, same-active-ingredient terms. Incumbents that are themselves a seller (FBN Direct, Nutrien) will not surface a competitor's lower quote; that is a conflict of interest baked into their P&L. An independent agent that does not sell the inputs can.

**3. Rebate and prepay/contract auditing.** Crop-protection retail runs on manufacturer rebate/program pricing (programs average ~25% of a manufacturer's selling price, 50%+ on some products), so the "real" net price only appears after year-end ([CropLife](https://www.croplife.com/management/marketingrebate-programs/)) (vendor-channel-sourced, directional (estimate)). Tools that decode this exist only on the retailer side (AgVend's Program Management helps the seller capture rebates). Nobody audits it for the grower. Our agent reconciles every invoice against every tier, threshold, and early-fill milestone to catch under-credited rebates, and models prepay timing against price risk, storage, tax deductibility, and the working-capital window. Incumbents will not build the grower-side audit because it directly reduces the margin they earn from opacity.

**4. Negotiation.** This is the one agentic capability already proven at scale, just not in ag. Pactum's autonomous negotiation has covered $8B+ in spend for Walmart and Maersk; in the Walmart deployment ~64-68% of approached suppliers reached agreement, averaging ~3% savings plus ~35-day term improvements, on a gain-share fee ([Thunderbird/ASU](https://thunderbird.asu.edu/thought-leadership/journals-case-series/case-series-listing/pactums-ai-contract-negotiations)). The agent gives growers comparable-farm price anchors and fires RFQs / reverse auctions to local dealers, the way Keelvar runs sourcing for volatile commodities. The gain-share model (pay only from realized savings) is perfect for a skeptical operator and aligns our incentives with the farmer's. Horizontal players (Coupa, SAP Ariba, Pactum) will not target the farm because ag's rebate/prepay/zone-pricing structure is too idiosyncratic to be worth their enterprise focus.

**5. Auto-PO and delivery tracking.** Once a price is agreed, the agent prepares a one-tap purchase order and tracks the shipment to the ranch. The rails exist: Ever.Ag's "Roger" already runs agentic AI for ag freight execution, and agentic payment standards (OpenAI + Stripe's Agentic Commerce Protocol, Visa Intelligent Commerce, Mastercard Agent Pay) are arriving. We ride these rather than build them.

**6. Spend-versus-budget control across entities and accounts.** A Batth-scale operation (~183 meters, ~57 PG&E accounts, 6 legal entities) has input spend fragmented across many dealer and co-op accounts with no single ledger. The agent gives one legible view of every SKU, dealer, ranch, and entity, with spend tracked against the season budget. This is the identical multi-entity legibility problem Terra already solves for PG&E energy, which is why we are uniquely positioned: the data model and the customer are the same.

**Why incumbents structurally can't or won't match this.** Three reasons, in order. (a) Conflict of interest: FBN and the dealer channel are sellers, so an honest cross-supplier scout and a grower-side rebate audit work against their own margin. An independent agent has no such conflict. (b) Opacity is their business model: the rebate/zone-pricing/prepay maze is a feature for retailers (it lets them price-discriminate by farm size and sophistication), so they will not build the tool that dissolves it. (c) Focus: horizontal procurement AI is not built for the farm, and the ag incumbents are row-crop and national-average centric, leaving California specialty and permanent crops under-served.

A necessary honesty check on the AI thesis: full B2B autonomy is being walked back industry-wide toward "AI-assisted workflows," fewer than 25% of B2B suppliers use agentic AI today, and only ~27% of consumers trust fully autonomous AI to make financial transactions (a cross-industry analogue, not farm-specific) ([DigitalCommerce360](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2026/03/10/agentic-commerce-faces-reality-check-in-b2b-ecommerce/)). Gartner's headline that AI agents will intermediate ~90% of B2B buying (~$15T) by 2028 is an aggressive forecast, not a measured fact (estimate) ([Gartner via DigitalCommerce360](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2025/11/28/gartner-ai-agents-15-trillion-in-b2b-purchases-by-2028/)). So the agent ships human-in-the-loop by default: it forecasts, scouts, audits, and negotiates, then presents a one-tap PO with dollar caps and advisor visibility. True delegated auto-buy is a later setting, not the day-one product.

## Positioning & One-Liner

**The purchasing agent that buys your farm's inputs for less, and shows you exactly how much it saved.**

Positioned as the farmer's independent agent, not a store. FBN says "buy from us and save." We say "we work for you, we scout everyone including the people who already have your business, and you only pay us from what we save you." Where FBN is a row-crop marketplace, we are a California-specialty-grade spend agent that already understands your ranches, entities, and (via Tool 1) your energy.

## Wedge & Why Now

The wedge is price legibility on the grower's own spend: ingest the farm's actual invoices, normalize every line to a per-unit, same-active-ingredient market band, and show "you overpaid by $X." Same play as Terra's energy tool (make the mess legible, then find the money), now on the largest controllable cost on the farm. Legibility first earns the right to optimize and, eventually, to act.

Why now:

- **Margins are negative,** so willingness to act on savings is at a peak (2025 per-acre margins negative for every major row crop; net farm income down ~22% from 2022 to 2024, ~$182B to ~$140.7B) ([AFBF](https://www.fb.org/market-intel/declining-farm-economy-continues-to-pressure-profitability)).
- **Working capital is strained,** so timing and rebate dollars matter more than ever (2025 operating loans ~30% larger YoY, first-year interest up 70-90%, Chapter 12 bankruptcies up 46% to 315) ([KC Fed](https://www.kansascityfed.org/agriculture/agfinance-updates/larger-operating-loans-boost-farm-lending-activity-in-2025/)).
- **Agentic commerce rails are arriving** (ACP, Visa/Mastercard agent payments, Ever.Ag freight agents), so the auto-PO and delivery legs are now buildable rather than hypothetical.
- **FBN itself is contracting,** leaving the specialty and permanent-crop segment, and the independent (non-seller) position, open.
- **Policy tailwind:** a bipartisan Fertilizer Transparency Act would force USDA to publish weekly prices, validating the transparency thesis at the policy level ([Agri-Pulse](https://www.agri-pulse.com/articles/24432-house-bill-aims-to-provide-fertilizer-price-transparency)).

## How This Fits Terra

Terra is an operating system for California farmers. Tool 1 is the PG&E energy tool: it makes a grower's energy account legible (all meters, rates, cycles, solar) and surfaces the money hiding in it. The purchasing agent is the natural Tool 2 because it is the same thesis applied to the next-largest controllable cost, sold to the same customer, on the same data model.

- **Same legibility-first thesis.** Energy made legible, then optimized; inputs made legible, then optimized. The retrospective-before-predictive, close-the-loop, one-recommendation-equals-one-action grammar carries over directly.
- **Same shared data model.** Farm, Entity, Ranch/Block, Pump, Crop, and the Recommendation grammar (`situation + action + impactUsd + one-tap response + after-the-fact result`, shaped to be executable) already support an input PO as an action type. The data model was designed so this move is mechanical, and so the monorepo split when Tool 2 starts is just moving files.
- **Same customer, deeper wallet.** The Batth-scale grower already trusts Terra with energy; inputs are a larger, adjacent spend that no incumbent makes legible across their entities.
- **Cross-tool leverage.** The crop/irrigation plan that drives energy modeling is the same plan that yields the input bill of materials. Terra is the only player positioned to derive input demand from a farm it already understands.

The discipline from Tool 1 holds: plain operator language (blocks, sets, acres, dealers, not "SKU velocity"), mobile-first for the farmer in a truck, respect the trusted-advisor circle (the PCA and dealer rep are in it nearly 100% of the time, so the agent arms the grower and their advisor rather than trying to replace the human), and never lead with a screaming money number, the farm known at a glance leads.

## Honest Risks

- **Channel conflict with dealers and co-ops.** The Big Seven retailers control ~70% of crop inputs and have deep local agronomist relationships; switching costs are largely relational (farmers rate the salesperson relationship ~6.8-7.0 on a 9-point scale, above the company). An agent that scouts around the dealer can trigger incumbent hostility (FBN faced exactly this, including a now-closed Canadian Competition Bureau probe). Mitigation: position as the grower's agent that can include their existing dealer's quote, arming rather than bypassing the trusted advisor.
- **Trust to delegate spend.** This is the steepest climb. Only ~27% of consumers trust fully autonomous AI for financial transactions (cross-industry, not farm-specific (estimate)), and 53% of North American farmers are "very concerned" about clear ROI before buying ag-tech. Mitigation: prove retrospective accuracy on past invoices first, then predicted-vs-actual close-the-loop, then human-in-the-loop one-tap with dollar caps; true auto-buy comes much later.
- **Data access.** The model needs the grower's invoices, dealer accounts, and crop plan. Farmers distrust handing receipts and field data to a VC-backed third party (a documented FBN complaint). Mitigation: legibility-for-the-grower framing, transparent data use, and never storing utility or financial credentials in a way the agent or repo can read (the Tool 1 credential discipline applies).
- **Supplier liquidity.** A scouting/negotiation agent is only as good as the breadth of suppliers willing to quote; marketplace value depends on retailer-network density, which is thin for California specialty outside the Midwest row-crop footprint that GROWERS and FBN built around. Mitigation: start where Terra already has customer density and a wedge (the existing California specialty base), and lead with the rebate-audit and price-benchmark value, which deliver dollars even before a deep live-quote network exists.
- **Thin-margin trap.** Ag marketplaces have repeatedly failed to find product-market fit, and the cautionary tales (Indigo Ag's reported ~94% markdown to a disputed ~$200M from a ~2021 peak of ~$3.5B (estimate), Silo's lending blowup) all over-promised a thin-margin "tech-enabled" trade as high-margin tech. Mitigation: the durable winners attach a money-moving rail (transparency + financing/rebate-share/gain-share) to the data and stay capital-disciplined; lead with software legibility and gain-share savings, not balance-sheet-heavy logistics or lending on day one.

## Product Definition

Terra's purchasing agent is an AI-native, farmer-side input procurement agent for California specialty and permanent-crop growers. It ingests a farm's crop and irrigation plan and its historical input invoices, forecasts a per-product bill of materials and buying calendar, makes every line of input spend legible against a normalized per-unit market band, then actively scouts live prices across dealers, co-ops, and generic-equivalent channels, audits manufacturer rebates and prepay contracts against the grower's actual invoices, negotiates on the grower's behalf on a gain-share basis, and prepares a one-tap, human-approved purchase order with delivery tracking and spend-versus-budget control across every entity and account. It is the grower's independent agent, not a store: it copies FBN's proven price-transparency wedge and direct-sourcing model, and beats it on the dimensions FBN structurally cannot follow, autonomous demand forecasting, conflict-free cross-supplier scouting, grower-side rebate auditing, and agentic negotiation, all built on Terra's existing shared data model so it ships as the natural Tool 2 alongside the PG&E energy tool. It launches human-in-the-loop with dollar caps and advisor visibility, earning the right to act by first proving retrospective accuracy and closing the predicted-versus-actual loop, exactly as Terra's energy tool does.
